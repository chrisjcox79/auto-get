22 Aug 2018 18:20

I wish I felt intrigue rather than confusion. I like a good  vibe, I would spend hours some nights looking at some of the most mysterious unsolved cases the world has to offer back in the day. This is clearly building off of that foundation, and it starts off so promising.

But it never capitalizes on it. It never gives that thing that I can sink my teeth into, that one aspect that weaves the bits into that captivating net that catches my intrigue. We get these journals that are supposed to tell a story but end up too disjointed to truly follow, and even if we could they might be utterly meaningless. Part of it might be that the researchers never really connect, I didn't really get invested in them, certainly not the writer. It seems clear that the video footage is supposed to be the payoff, but I don't really get anything from it, either because the build-up didn't clearly lead towards it or because again we're lead to feel like it's potentially unreliable. The whole  thing sort of kills the intrigue, because then we don't even really know what the story is.

It is a shame, because the fuel is there. This has the potential to reach that  vibe. It just isn't there right now.

I liked it, actually. I admit that it did take me a second, and I got the first inklings of an idea of what might be going on, and you just built on it over time.

My take away was this, and I think the writer was trying to clue us in, as well; the wire was falling into a  as they put it. The lack of existence is what lies on the other side of the door. It seems as though anything that passes through is erased from the memories of everyone left alive. All of the redundant documents and repeated requests have been fulfilled and carried out, but every test completed would wipe your memory of completing the test.

The only pill I find hard to swallow is the idea that the Foundation wouldn't have caught on, though. I doubt they'd let things go this badly for such an extended period of time before realizing that something wasn't quite right.
