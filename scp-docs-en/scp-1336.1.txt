Unknown elements...

03 May 2012 08:47

Using  118. The gaps between Hydrogen and Helium don't indicate that there's something in between, they're there so that elements which behave similarly share columns (hydrogen with the alkali metals, helium with the noble gases). Elements above Iron (Fe, 26) are atomically unstable, tending to decay over time inexorably toward Iron, releasing energy as they do. The time it takes for them to decay goes down precipitously the higher the atomic number, with the highest ones there having half-lives measurable only in fractions of a millisecond, and only ever created (as far as we know) with immense apparati of particle accelerators and fusors.

Chemical compounds involving these elements are extraordinarily unlikely (due to the high energy necessary to create them, ionic and covalent bonds would be highly improbable to form, and by the time the energy levels could possibly decrease, the atoms in question would've decayed into something else). Any new element on the periodic table would have to be an even higher atomic number, hence even faster-decaying, hence even less likely to make a compound, ever. The only way that could work is to theorize that for some reason, once you get past the series of elements which decay faster and faster, there's some point at which you hit an  in the ridiculously high atomic numbers. All the evidence thus far provides no support to the hypothesis that such an island exists.

If you want to impress upon the reader that this SCP knows about compounds the Foundation can't duplicate, there are more plausible ways of going about it. Complex compounds involving noble gases, for example, are extremely rare: Neon has no known neutral compounds, helium has only a few hypothetical ones, and the rest have only a few hundred possible compounds combined. Making compounds with those would require tremendous technical proficiency, advanced chemistry knowledge, and skillful manipulation, while still being plausible.

This is also technically inaccurate. Elements with high atomic numbers are not unstable due to the circumstances of their creation, but due to interactions of the strong and weak forces on the nucleus. This is borne out experimentally, as when we fuse hydrogen into helium (involving the same incredibly high energy levels), the resulting helium is just as stable as helium found naturally. Further, astrophysics research supports the notion that all elements heavier than hydrogen were originally formed by stellar fusion processes, operating in the cores of older, larger stars which had run out of hydrogen to fuse and instead fused helium into boron, carbon, oxygen, silicon, and so on up to iron. When these stars novaed (or supernovaed), these elements (and even heavy elements like uranium) were scattered throughout the cosmos, to eventually gravitationally accrete into planets made up chiefly of elements such as iron and silicon. Despite their formation in the hearts of a star, at millions of degrees Celsius, with pressures measurable in millions or billions of atmospheres, the atoms formed in these reactions have been stable enough for Terra to be here for 4.5 billion years and counting. The circumstances of formation are not the determining factor in atomic stability and nuclear decay, the mass of the nucleus is.
