It's sad that we'll never know what made that large snake so special.

+block

–block

Onto business: I want to get into this, but there are so many weird bits that seem inconsistent or don't go anywhere that I can't feel it's very well-constructed, unless I'm just misinterpreting the whole thing.

If a single instance can reproduce into several hundred instances via high winds, why does the other reproduction method happen, and why haven't they taken over the world already?

These buildings will be disassembled by fibrous fungus, and the materials apparently used to produce structures such as hotels, motels, boarding homes, condominiums, and small businesses with residences.
附加记录：    

Okay, so they build things. Where? Just, on the ground in the middle of the city? Are the things they build also 2379 instances, or do they just arrive, turn buildings into different buildings, and then leave? Why? Why do the containment procedures make no note of how to contain an outbreak of moving buildings that attack cities?

Once the SCP-2379 infestation has used all the available construction materials, it will begin moving towards the next largest urban population center and repeat the process. There is no known upper limit on the size that SCP-2379 infestations can reach.
附加记录：    

So, once it starts attacking cities, it'll just keep attacking cities. Do the people trapped inside just die of thirst? Again, you never specify whether or not they're using buildings to build more of themselves. It seems to be implied, but then that raises several additional questions, since the first half of the description implies that they're made of fungus but they're just home-shaped.

Are they entirely fungus or are they actual homes made of plaster and metal, just with fungus inside? If the former, how/why are they building more buildings? If the latter, how do they disappear in high winds?

And, if they go on a city-disassembling spree, why are there no specific containment procedures on how to deal with that? At the very least, that's something that would receive immediate national attention, and then probably a very extensive attack and coverup operation.

I want to like the idea here, but there are just so many issues in logic here that I'm left confused as to what these things are or do.

I'm conflicted, on this one.

On the one hand, it's very strange, it's well-written, and I like the origin, and the advancement is neat. There is an odd moment where you jump from , and I'm not really sure how that gap is bridged. Like, people move into the squishy house voluntarily?

On the other side there are a lot of details in the article that come off as a pretty shitty commentary about poor people. I don't think this was your intent at all, and I don't think it stands out as a through line since they were always interspersed with other descriptions, but they still happened often enough to be very uncomfortable. Examples:

- The items being destroyed by tornadoes and hurricanes used as a method of dispersion (in retrospect, they're probably inhabited when this occurs)

- People refusing to leave the objects due to (fictional) opiate addiction

- People in these communities becoming highly insular and growing their own food

- People in these communities stealing from neighboring communities

- These commodities destroying existing structures and producing strip malls, motels, and other urban sprawl

So, I don't think that it was your intention to consciously invoke stereotypes about poverty. I fully believe you just set out to write a weird creepy skip. Unfortunately, it comes off as a skip that could take credit for real-world behaviour, but defines that behaviour is a really negative way.

Edit: It really doesn't help that this is a compulsion effect. It reads like,  is that a necessary storyline?
