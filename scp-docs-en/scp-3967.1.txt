“communards” is an inherently funny word, and should be normalized

18 Sep 2020 18:52

This is an article about the intersection of hippies and computers in the late 1960’s. Despite the popular image of hippies as a bunch of pot-smoking luddites, there was a strain of hippie thought, particularly among the “Back to the Earth” communards that emphasized the emancipatory potential of computers and cybernetics. It had a huge influence on the thinking of what eventually became the internet, and after that, Silicon Valley. Reality’s weird sometimes.

This entire article was basically an excuse to force myself to read a book that’s been on my shelf for a while (From Counterculture to Cyberculture: Stewart Brand, the Whole Earth Network, and the Rise of Digital Utopianism by Fred Turner). It may seem like I was laying it on a bit thick with Woody's letters, but if anything, I was downplaying the deep strangeness of letters published in the Whole Earth Catalog the Mother Earth Journal.

The Only Game In Town canon was conceived of as an extension of the description of the Hanged King in the Wanderers’ Library.

The being currently known as the Hanged King was at one time a resident of the Library. However, during its residence, the Library grew more and more insular, more and more depressive in its scope. These problems finally reached their apex toward the end of the 11th century, when a huge amount of knowledge was suddenly rent from the Library and lost. The fear of knowledge and personal growth in the outside world reached a height that took centuries to repair, and the remnants of these feelings continue even today.

The Hanged King itself is an ancient, unknowable thing with more names than the stars. The ancient Babylonian manuscripts from which it grew were destroyed, but there are more and more instances of its influence appearing. Some regard it as the original source of fear to appear in written words, while others claim it is far older.

Votes don't help the author, so I've vowed to always leave a comment with mine, at the very least whenever I rate an article negatively. If you feel the same, help out by leaving critique with more of your votes. Comment follows:

I don't like seeing non-international specifics in articles -  without specifying (I assume) US rubs me the wrong way. It's also confusing because it's shortly followed by a British-style date.

I thought the letters were good. I expected the plot to be 'computer goes wrong' generally, but the cards don't necessarily prompt the community's actions - I like the thought that they're just like that, and the computer was either reflecting that or taking advantage of it.

The crosslink to 701 didn't mean anything to me, so it didn't work as a punchline.

I don't think the SCP itself was used as much as it could have been. It's a premise that works in any setting, not exclusively the setting that the story takes place in, and it might have been interesting to see what advice it had to give once it had been contained. I can appreciate that the story was about the setting and not the SCP, though, but if that's the case I wonder why the cards are included at all and not handwaved away as infohazardous. The story doesn't seem to require the SCP at all, beyond a couple of explicit mentions, and the two feel disconnected. I think I may have missed a point here, so it's a novote overall.

As a contest entry, I don't see how this relates to the previous entry, nor do I see how it sets up another - but I've no reason to assume that this team's entries form a series at all. Equally, this entry doesn't require any background knowledge, which is definitely in its favour.
