Added a dinky little image I whipped up using basic paint effects until I can land something better.

Edit: I’ve had quite a few rates without the needle moving much, so I suppose there’s some division here. While it’s obviously not obligatory, I welcome any and all comments, including from those who don’t like it. Since I’m a greenlighter, it’s important for me to have an idea of what isn’t working in my own article.

Edit 2: I didn't want to do this initially, but I think some people are downvoting because the big answer behind this that makes things click isn't explicitly stated, and while a couple of people have gotten it, I'm getting the impression that many people are not. So here's what's behind the redactions and such:

The Big Secret 

Individuals (and their instances) become smaller and more compact (so very small, so very close) as they approach the center, they would appear from the outside to be a light. Just as well, those tiny, tiny, tiny instances would then become the dust itself, meaning they were wiping people from their camera lenses (hence the biological makeup of the dust). It would be redacted by the Foundation because the ethics of that whole situation would likely complicate the relatively easy containment they're managing already.

I was inspired by 2719 to write a  that had a definite answer, but required a bit of work to figure it out. Maybe it's too much. Either way, if this answer helps makes everything make more sense, I hope it might be reevaluated with the knowledge that I wasn't using the black boxes to escape questions I didn't know the answer to.

Edit 2: Removed some redactions that seemed to be more trouble that they were worth.

Are either of them original?
Alright, at the end. I really dislike the movement of Dr. Mai to the new SCP. While I completely expected her to be disciplined for her behavior, I can't believe that after showing that she couldn't handle this job where she lost 2 D-class, she would be put into an apparently dangerous position in charge of MORE D-Class. It's like immersion therapy but it could actually harm the patient AND other people. Not to mention is seems cruel rather than cold. The foundation has tons of positions, and typically when I read someone being moved because of a similar situation they are moved to a place where they don't have as much chance of fucking things up. Pushing this woman like they seem to do will likely not make her  (What?!), but make her less stable, possibly turning her into a security risk. Give her a damn desk job if you have to, move her to a quiet scp, hell stick her with 999 for a week and some therapy for awhile and reevaluate her personality.

Anyways, while there isn't anything particularly special about this scp, there doesn't need to be. I like the premise, I like the way you go about things sometimes, but overall I end up a bit on the negative side. I think you can make this into an article that I would upvote however, and I don't want you to think I hated it. I actually enjoyed reading it except for the last part.

I know this was a wall of mostly negative text, sorry about that. But take it as you grabbing my interest enough for me to care this much. Also, feel free to point out any errors in my understanding.

Re: Promising, but negative for now

09 Dec 2019 00:09

1) I think that they’re happier saying, “Those people are gone, there are no Foundation personnel inside the SCP” than they are admitting that there might be living people who could conceivably be rescued. They don’t exactly know what happens in there per se, but they aren’t interested in debating whether they should attempt to extract them, which is exactly what Mai tried to do.

2) From the Foundation’s view, this wouldn’t be cruel. She has to learn to deal with this to succeed in the Foundation; they can’t simply have her as an ethical live wire. They have justified reasons for using expendable people in tests and maintenance. Mai comes from “the real world” where this would be absurd, but the Foundation doesn’t work by regular ethics. They need her to be comfortable sending people to their deaths, and perhaps forcing her to do it over and over would sharpen her into the researcher they want. It’s absurd to us, but it would be fairly logical for them, even if we might disagree whether it’s effective or not.

3) This is largely the reason why I didn’t make a direct 001 connection. It’s simply implied that it’s something big and scary, or at least 99 believes so. Exactly what that is might be the subject of an established or new SCP, but this article focuses on the “safe room” that seems to exist to counter it.

4) I didn’t mean to imply that the Foundation has any desire to use this SCP. They don’t know exactly what it is. They know that 99, or something claiming to be 99, thinks it’s a safe place, but they’re not going to march anyone else in there.

Hope this clears a few things up. I appreciate you working through your doubts with me. I’m getting the impression that others might be struggling to piece the puzzle together as well. I wrote this for people like me who love these SCPs that make you work a bit for the answer.

Edit: I removed the redacted sentence about the byproduct. It’s causing more problems than it’s worth.

1) I imagine the D-class are the same for me as anyone else; they’re expendable test subjects. But they’re still people. I didn’t want to use an MTF or agent to explore an unknown SCP with no clear threat, but I did want someone who could operate a bit more competently. Hence, the PET: a team of two D-class who have survived more than a couple of other SCPs and seem to have a rapport with their researcher. I wanted to show what happens when the Foundation fails to properly indoctrinate their doctors into treating humans inhumanely. It’s not so much a war as it is simply a doctor who ended up caring for a couple of her test subjects.

2) The redactions seem to be contentious. The reason is that if they acknowledge the possibility that there are living people inside, that might obligate them in some way. As it stands, they can’t confirm that anyone is actually alive in there, or that there aren’t hostiles. A rescue mission would be ill-advised. But they don’t even have to deal with the controversy; they can simply claim that if they don’t know for sure, then they might as well keep the theory away from the rank and file to prevent any complaints about just leaving it alone.

Hope this clarifies. I’m really wrestling with the redactions if that’s what’s turning people off to this, but after I had a couple of people in chat guess the right answer for what was happening, I really wanted to keep this a little bit of a justified puzzle.

Thanks for asking, I appreciate it.
