It isn't, at least not yet. It's the sort of thing that navies would play with (probably before other military branches), since having sufficient power and space to mess with prototype technology isn't usually a problem on ships. And I'm fairly sure most of the work they're doing (in as much as they publicly acknowledge such things) is built around countering radar rather than visual light. Cloaking devices as we think of them in fiction simply do not exist, and the closest analogues that do are sufficiently imperfect that you'll notice the hidden object if it is against a solid, not-textured background (like a clear sky). They also require obscene amounts of power, which would make an application of the sort described in the containment procedures highly impractical (and expensive).

Further, they would not prevent there from being a shadow from the camouflaging structure itself, which at sunrise and sunset could easily be kilometers long. Given the terrain in Katavi National Park, the Foundation has built a 200 meter tall sundial that any satellite overpass or airplane flying at standard cruising altitude for several hundred kilometers around would have no trouble seeing. (Which is another point - a 200 meter tall structure presents an aviation hazard; most places in the United States require appropriate warning lights on structures taller than 200 feet, and the International Civil Aviation Organization has global standards which most countries follow.) It's just simply not possible to make something that tall which can't be noticed by someone with an appropriate vantage point. A better solution would be to come up with some plausible structure of appropriate height (a watchtower for wildfire prevention, a radio tower, etc., though 200 meters is a bit tall for most things) and explain it away that way.

Though I've stated this in PMs, for the record, I basically agree with this assessment, both in the broad sense and in the specifics. (I am also declining to vote for the time being.)

There are interesting things that can be done with the thinking-talking-rock idea - and it most certainly is an interesting idea, at least in theory - but I personally think that the civilization-ending-eldritch-abominations-of-doom is one of the weaker ones you could run with, especially the more explicitly you choose to do so. (I sometimes suspect that 1050 is rated as high as much in spite of that aspect as because of it.)

Remember the old adage about showing rather than telling. If you tell me  I probably won't believe you and almost certainly won't be scared. If you show me what looks like a ghost in the room, I'll be more inclined to believe you, but it'd have to be pretty convincing to scare me. If you convince me that I keep seeing something out of the corner of my eye and hear the faint rustle of movement behind me, in spite of my not being able to find anything there, and suggest the last owner of the house disappeared under mysterious circumstances, then it is far more likely that you'll have set me up to scare myself. (This is hard, which is why so many horror films use gore and jump-scares to such an extent.) So, if you can take that sort of logic and apply it to civilization-ending-things, then you're in business. The obvious flipside being that if you are too subtle, it will go right over some of your audience's heads, but that's a different problem. Unfortunately, in my opinion you haven't struck that balance.

Bottom line, I think the concept has lots of potential, but I recommend either pursuing a different direction from hiding-from-civilization-enders completely, or at the very least aiming to make that part more implicit than explicit (think ).

The problem, ooblex, is that if you want the reader to not take 2929-1 at its word, then you need some indication in the article to suggest that they shouldn't.

To put it bluntly, you're asking us to be credulous towards the fact that there is a globe-spanning, top secret organization which can make convincing-to-the-point-of-being-an-invisibility-cloak holograms that are hundreds of meters tall, and that there is a kilometers-long piece of obsidian that constantly alters its surface to create patterns clear enough to be interpreted as language (apparently generated coherently by an intelligence other than a regular old human being), and then you're asking us to be incredulous as to what this giant rock-that-writes is saying - even to the point of questioning whether or not the giant rock-that-writes is in fact anything other than pure random chance which just so happens to create symbols that correspond with an intelligible dialogue on the part of the researchers. So, would you like us to suspend disbelief, or not? The reader is likely to follow both Occam's Razor (the simplest explanation is best in the absence of evidence to the contrary) and Law of Conservation of Detail (writers rarely use up valuable space to mention irrelevant details, and the shorter the work of fiction, the less likely any particular detail is to be a product of random chance and/or not plot relevant).

It is true that we (both the Doylist readers and Watsonian members of the Foundation) have little corroborating evidence to suggest we ought to take 2929-1 at its word. Ten-fifty exists, and the fact that you've made a cross-link (about which I'm not complaining, mind you) gives some corroborating evidence. The fact that both give issue a warning, the shared previously known script, and that there is not an apparent shared origin (i.e., not both made by the same GoI) suggests that both should be given more serious consideration by researchers than they would be lacking that sort of corroboration. The difference is that the article for 1050 both includes details that indicate that all is not as it seems and includes clear delineations between what the Foundation knows (fact) vs thinks (educated guesses). While the article represents a prevailing viewpoint, others which are mutually exclusive exist which cannot be definitively debunked. All of your comments about how we should take 2929-1 with a grain of salt are in the meta-text, not the text itself. Votes reflect the article as a stand-alone piece of fiction, not (at least not necessarily) the commentary the author provides in the discussion thread.

This is a very valid point, one I had come across when I had first started my draft. I considered tying in 1, but I thought it might unnecessarily complicate things. I might, just might, put it in some time after I get back from my upcoming vacation.

A possibly Paleolithic individual, whose ancient precursor-Khoisan language almost certainly lacked a written form, communicates by transliterating its language in a  using binary code? A substitution of what — is it producing hexadecimal code for a Latin-alphabet phonetic spelling, or… what? It would be easier to believe if it just produced visible letters on its top surface.
附加记录：    

OK, OK. I know there is no  or even a written form for Khoisan languages, excluding modern phonetic attempts. I chose to leave this plot hole where is because, to be frank, a majority of readers probably haven't even heard of Khoisan languages (that is not an insult to anyone), much less know that they do not have writing systems. I felt it would be unnecessary to maneuver around the discrepancy and that it would distract me from more important issues, of which there were many. I applaud you for being the first person to realize this issue and dub thee the Khoisan Knight. Congrats. :P Also, please keep in mind the culture, if any, that -1 possibly belonged to was bewilderingly and anachronistically advanced. That is part of what is so anomalous and mysterious about its origins.

This message in binary is  to an unknown script? In what way?
附加记录：    

I dunno. I don't speak Khoisan. I assumed I would be granted some hand-waving for that.

Given the message , was the researchers' first thought to use gamma rays? Did they try radio first, or even just a big set of speakers?
附加记录：    

Good point. But due to how deep underground -1 actually is, gamma rays are really the only way to effectively transmit something to it (or at least the only one I could think of). This is the same reason why GRI was necessary.

Plan A is ? What is a camouflage projection? Can it only be seen from directly above? What are their plans to hide this object from seismologists around the world?
附加记录：    

It is more like Plan A is 

 device seen in Wikipedia article on active camouflage. It is pretty cool.

Basically, the structure is like a fence surrounding the perimeter for the exposed end of 2929, so it would not stop views from above.

The tremors associated with 2929 are too small to be considered worthy of research to any competent seismologist. Even if the seismologist is not competent, would a seismologist really go to the site of a small tremor to somehow investigate? I highly doubt it.

What is noteworthy about the numbers mentioned in footnotes 2 and 3?
附加记录：    

I wanted to further illustrate how perfectly crafted (if that is the appropriate word) 2929 is.

I was right there with you even despite some of the peculiar bits
附加记录：    

What peculiar bits in particular?

Find some other identity and origin for the subterranean mass of post-perovskite and I think you can reform it into a winner.
附加记录：    

The problem is, if I were to change the nature and origin of the subterranean mass of post-perovskite, how in the world would the Foundation be able to figure that out? Even in the article as it is, the Foundation doesn't really know what its nature is or its origins, the Foundation not knowing the latter of which I thought I made pretty clear with my final paragraph. The only things the Foundation has to go on are the patterns on the exposed surface of 2929, the GRI readings, and -1's vague and possibly false claims. The patterns tell them what -1 is  the GRI readings tell them the shape, size, and composition of 2929 and -1, and -1's claims cannot be taken as truth for certain. So the Foundation can only speculate its nature and origins, and nothing more.

I hope this helps you to see the article from a different perspective. Thanks for the input.
