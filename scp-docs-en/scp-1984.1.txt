Fold

25 Jun 2012 08:46

I really want to upvote this, as I feel the idea has a lot of potential, but some things bother me:

First of all, making it humanoid. In and of itself this is not a deal breaker, but it feels kind of out of place. When it's revealed to be a ghost, though, that kind of puts it off for me.

Second of all, how is it supposed to be an effective nuclear deterrent if its speed is so limited and it can only launch nukes from one facility at a time? The real Dead Hand, which could launch all of Russia's nukes at once, seems way more efficient.

Third, this thing can obviously comprehend advanced ideas, so has anyone tried to explain to it that the Cold War is over and the USSR no longer exists?

Fourth, if it only effects that one type of nuclear missile, couldn't it be neutralized by simply decommissioning all of those missiles (and, if necessary, transferring their payloads into a different type of missile?)

Fold

25 Jun 2012 09:17

1. Not sure how to address that one.

2. The intent was to address Dead Hand's weakness; it still needed to be activated by a living, breathing human. Many people doubted its existence for a long time because the idea of a fully automated doomsday machine, even one that needed to be switched  how effective a deterrent is it if there's any doubt that you'll actually put it online? This thing is already dead, and full of unending malice. It's not human anymore, and thus can be a reliable threat in a way a person never could be. Whether that works, I can't say.

And frankly, the  it's not going to die in any aftereffects of a nuclear exchange. This, of course, depends on the supposition that the Russians have done a good job keeping enemy intelligence on all of their silos to a minimum.

3. In my vision of this thing's intelligence, it has a warped . Which of course would set it off.

4. Someone with more technical knowledge than myself can correct me here, but I think that it's really, REALLY difficult to completely overhaul your ICBM technology and redeploy. The Russians would not want to scrap the backbone of their nuclear arsenal if they could help it (after all, they still need to maintain a deterrent to this day), and they have faith in the Foundation to keep this thing contained.

I hope this provides a little more context, and thanks for taking the time to comment!
