Imagine it this way. Someone gives you a small cardboard box and tells you that opening it would kill you. They can't tell you why or how. They just tell you not to open it. So you put the box away somewhere safe and don't open it. From that point on, so long as you leave it alone, it doesn't matter if the box actually will or will not kill you; it's rendered harmless by you leaving it alone.

Soyou have no reason to poke and prod at it to find out whether the box is actually dangerous, and every reason to not investigate further, just in case it does actually turn out to be dangerous.

This skip is basically Pascal's wager. It might be harmless, but it might be an dangerous disease that could wipe out humanity if it got out into the wild. Why take that risk when it's not costing all that much to keep it contained?

What if testing got approved, and in the process of testing it was confirmed that the disease is real and infection got into a researcher, and from there into more Foundation staff, and perhaps even further? Would you expect the higher ups to sign off on that risk when there's nothing to be gained from it except for a small cost saving?

Scientists may despise the unknown and want to study what the Foundation gets hold of, but my personal headcanon is that they take backseat to the mission: Secure, contain, protect.

And this thing is secure. It's contained. It's protected. The job is done. Further study can't make it safer, but could only potentially make it more dangerous. I don't think they'd ever sign off on further study unless compelling evidence came to light to make the risk justifiable. And as further study is needed to get that evidence, it makes it a Catch-22. You're absolutely right that if the researchers were allowed to study this, they'd discover the truth about whether it is or is not a ruse (remember, it could be either: the document written by the D-Class might not be accurate)but they are being prevented from doing the studying.

That's my thinking anyway. There'd be a lot of frustrated scientists and researchers being told  by those in charge.

In short, I imagine the guy in charge of signing off on your request will say: 

(and I think I've just got the idea for my next skip :P)
