You're actually spot on. Its distance to the earth is proportionate to the distance between Earth and Sun. The change in height is proportional: Earth is, at its closest, 147,100,993km away from the Sun. Then, it moves away by, roughly, 5,000,000km. I used the relationship between those, and extrapolated that to the center of the earth to the position of the sphere. 0.03% of the distance between the center of the earth and the position of the sphere is roughly 2500 meters.

Do you think the article would benefit from the calculation? Some of the people I talked to figured it would not be interesting for most readers, as it doesn't add much. But the downside is that we do end up here, where you - and probably others - might be unclear on how its height/distance works.

Also, for fun

On the average, one revolution of the Earth relative to perihelion is about 25 minutes longer than one revolution relative to the December solstice. Perihelion advances one full calendar date every 60 or so years.
附加记录：    

During the late Mexica period, it would have fallen a few days after solstice, placing the sphere clearly in that area.

In the 1st or 2nd century BC, it would have fallen in the first few days of December, probably right near where you have it in India.

However, 4500 years ago, perihelion would have fallen about 61 days before December solstice (equivalent of October 21st if there had been such a thing as October back then). Which would have put the sphere more around 10.5-11° N according to a calculator I found.

South Sudan, or Nicaragua, or Vietnam, in which case they could also be anomalously tall :p

Interestingly (to me at least) the sidereal year (revolution around the sun relative to fixed stars) is about 21 minutes longer than the tropical year (time between solstices/equinoxes) and about 4 minutes shorter than the anomalistic year (time between perihelions/aphelions).
