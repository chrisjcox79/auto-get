I knew I was forgetting something, edited my first post to include thanks to the people who helped me.

As for Dr. Warm's contention, let me try to explain what my thinking was there. The sole reason for its inclusion was to show how incredibly difficult it is to test a thing whose anomalous property is that it can't be observed; I went into way more detail on this in my first draft before smarter people than me noted that it really made the article drag, so I cut most of that stuff out. I wasn't trying to go full on SCP-055 (which does that whole thing better than I ever could) but I did at least want to hint at how tough it is to figure out creative ways to actually figure out the parameters of this anomaly.

Also, I had it in my mind that pretty much everyone at the Foundation immediately assumed it was memetic in nature and that Dr. Warm was just coming up with a fringe idea, which is why I used the word . The point I was making was that it took 22 separate tests before they could actually conclusively disprove it despite it being a fairly goofy idea. That's how hard it was to test.

If all that doesn't make sense then I'll remove the line. If it makes sense but doesn't come across in the article then I'll figure out a way to rewrite it so it's clearer.

I'm thinking it's a little bit of a Schrodinger's Box effect, and partly exploiting the fact that there are mechanical limits to the non-observation effect. It doesn't directly detect observe people- only the weight of the box-o-unknown-contents it's attached to- and it doesn't report that information to anything but it's own cutoff switch. Basically it works for the same reason that the guy got trampled- the other people are still there as far as the laws of physics are concerned, you just can't directly perceive them or their actions.

Personally, I'd want to hook up a readout to the sensor, and see whether:

a) it would show you the actual weight of the elevator and contents1
b) it ceased to detect the other people and let the over-capacity elevator try to work2
or c) it showed you only your own weight but still functioned properly by not letting the elevator work when over capacity.

Footnotes
1. which would still tell you nothing definitive about the presence of other people, only that this was an exceptionally heavy elevator and that it may or may not change its weight randomly when you aren't looking.
2. because each person saw only their own weight, and none of them triggered the sensor individually.
