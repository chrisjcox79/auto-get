Effective worldbuilding in an article can be a beautiful thing. It can make the world of the SCP Foundation come to life and seem like a real place, with rules, history, and its own clashes and ideals. This is what makes pieces like WrongJohnSilver's 001 or the TwistedGears/djkaktus 001 so effective. Poor worldbuilding, on the other hand, typically ends up being exposition dumps. While they often do establish their own world, they bog themselves down and lose the reader in the resulting quagmire. I'm afraid this article was the latter for me.

+block

–block

The object itself is fairly interesting. A massive combat sub built into a giant hermit crab is quite a spectacle to behold, and I do like the first diagram explaining the various layers of the interior. This gives the article some depth and makes the object seem like an explored place. The second picture breaks my immersion a little, since I've seen that illustration around the net before, and I hesitate to believe it would be included in an SCP document.

Then we get down to the supporting documents. The News Articles after a while just blend into a long exposition dump. You have an entire civilization discovered and the only interesting/noteworthy documents the SCP Foundation can find are newspapers and maybe one or two other document styles? On top of that, the history you tell here is a little lukewarm to me as a reader. Perhaps I would find more fondness here if I was a little more invested in your Finnfolk canon, but as is going through those is a bit like watching a Star Wars movie and pausing every now and then to read an ongoing history about the Hutts. While I am sure that will draw some fans, it's not the reason I am here to see the film.

The exploration logs prove problematic in their own way. With dialogue gems like:

R-5: 

R-3: 

R-5: 

R-3: 
附加记录：    

R-2: 

R-Cap: 

R-5: 

R-Cap: 
附加记录：    

R-6: 

Matriarch:  [Five seconds of cackling.]

R-5: 
附加记录：    

These read more like the kind of things people might say to their friends while gathered around to play Dungeons and Dragons than believable dialogue from hardened MTF agents, or the elders of ancient and powerful civilizations. It just kills the immersion. On top of that, each of these logs reads like a tale. Had you made them their own tale series I think it might have worked out a little more in your favor, as then you could really get into imagery to describe your world and bring it to life. As is, however, the clinical tone and format of a log suck out that opportunity and we are just left with the dialogue and some dry description of actions.

Finally, you fall prey to Deus Ex Machina at the end. You build up the monsters of SCP-3700 quite a bit in that article and really do a good job justifying the Hiemal object class. Having one of them attack at the end here is a bold move, but instead of making use of the massive battle sub you have spent nearly 12K words building up, you have an outside force swoop in to save the day.

I really want to like this. I can tell a lot of blood, sweat, and tears was poured into it to bring it to life. Unfortunatley, the problems listed above just cause the whole thing to fail to solidfy, and the length does the rest to kill the experience. At +44 at the time of writing, its clear you have an audience here on the site for the article, but I don't think I'm part of it.

-1.

This criticism is fair and appreciated. I'm going to do my best to adjust and fix the pieces that you've pointed out made this less enjoyable. I've already made a massive change to the ending to remove the perceived Deus Ex Machina. As is, the recovered materials are not something I can fix as immediately, as I will have to carefully consider how to diversify and improve the individual pieces of recovered material.

ShowCriticism

HideResponse

In order to shed light on portions of this, I feel it necessary to explain some of the motivation behind pieces that were included. The recovered materials were constructed in order to give the reader space to breath between the logs. You are the first, though I'm sure not the only, person to leave feedback in a way that indicates this was problematic, and/or you didn't enjoy the story being woven by the logs, which is fair.

They are, somewhat heavily, leaning towards the side of exposition, which is a failure on my part. At the time that they were written, they were meant to serve the breathing room purpose and the primary vector for the history. I could perhaps have improved upon that structure, with more personalized documents and recovered materials, personal items, etc. Collected by the Foundation as they are exploring, to more indirectly/less expositionally demonstrate the events leading to the state the Finnfolk are currently in. This comes down to a lack of time, and a lack of necessary direction to see the exposition-heavy and lukewarm nature of the materials.

I understand the dialogue complaint, cause its a fairly common one on the site. I don't entirely agree with the criticism, especially in the face of a team of experienced agents who've, as to put it,  The characters are written the way they are because they're human. Because they've been through hell and how they cope with it, as a group that has chemistry, is by bantering. I went so far as to directly address this, in a not so subtle manner.

R-2: 

R-Cap: 
附加记录：    

The first example you provided was a suggestion made to inject more humor into the article, the second example has been praised by a number of different authors and users, and the last leads into a vicious response to a lack of respect on the part of R-5. I will probably do more editing to the most offending pieces of dialogue, but I'm relatively unwilling to budge on the bantering/somewhat loose and unprofessional nature of the Recon team members, or the eccentric behavior of Ragnhild, as those elements have received very positive responses from a spread of different users, and I'm afraid if I remove them, the logs will fall victim to the same issue the incident log from 3456 fell into.

With that said,

On top of that, each of these logs reads like a tale. Had you made them their own tale series I think it might have worked out a little more in your favor, as then you could really get into imagery to describe your world and bring it to life.
附加记录：    

This is the shortcoming of the medium, and obviously a pitfall of logs in general, but also a result of attempting to restrain my desire to describe every succinct detail (Knowing what is and isn't important, and being able to discern and prioritize elements over others is something I struggle with not just in writing, but everywhere. I've gotten into the habit of describing less rather than more). Several times, while I was giving feedback, Roget suggested small tweaks to give the reader a better visualization of what was happening/improve upon the imagery. I would love to know if there are specific spots or locations in the logs where I could do that in order to provide the reader with a better image of what is happening, and thus, better engage them. Clinical tone isn't the best medium for conveying imagery, but setting the stage in the right manner, its possible to paint a beautiful mental image, and any guidance therein where those images can be improved is appreciated.

Finally, in regards to the image, describing the nature of the nested cylinders proved extremely difficult to do without visual aides. I hate that it broke immersion for you, I was unsure how else I could help the reader visualize the space, and chose to include an image of what it would, theoretically, most closely resemble. If enough people found it immersion breaking I can certainly remove it.

I greatly appreciate the in-depth feedback, there are changes here that I can make and things I can improve on. I hate that you didn't enjoy it, and feel as if a number of these things I can fix and improve upon following the end of the 4000 contest. I've made significant efforts to fix the article's ending, and remove the Deus Ex-Machina, and replace it with a climactic battle, calling back to 3700, while demonstrating the capabilities of the machine that the entire article has built towards. It fixes one thing and hopefully, I can fix the rest.

This response got long, man, I just write too much.
