make my way back home when i learn to fly

17 May 2017 11:18

A skip about being stuck on aeroplanes, claustrophobia, and burning.

Yes, this is intended to tie in at least a little to Spoiler: she's forever trapped in transportation hell.

Grace Renfield is stolen from In Case You've Ever Wondered..., partially because it's an underrated tale and partially because of the thematic chords between an airplane filled with Miriam Wells clones and a woman who's a clone herself. Also because Nanku from Samsara would probably try to murder her fellow passengers.

Rambling noises below:

A quick Google tells me that flights on average fly at an altitude of 10 kilometers, as opposed to the extremely large 270. An altitude of 270 kilometers would mean you've gone outside the thermosphere, i.e. you're in space now, kids. There's something very wrong with whatever space Renfield's found herself in.
Depending on how long the flight is, I could handwave it as the other passengers thinking she's just fallen asleep on a red-eye flight. Until they land and then she doesn't wake up, at which point in time the awkwardness of waking a stranger up is overcome by the fact said stranger might be dead.
It'll activate when you spend 2 hours at the gate before catching a flight there.

+Spoilers

–block

The reason for all the burning and the smoke on the plane is that Miriam Wells is in the transportation afterlife. From long desert roads to airports, Miriam's forever stuck in the liminal spaces: the bits between the start and the end of your journey.

To repent for whatever she did to incur this punishment, she takes up penance: serving other people as part of her job as every single attendant on the flight. But for those who might find themselves in transportation hell because of what they did, whether that be take the wrong road or board the wrong flight, she'll act as a warning for to the troublemakers about what they might see in the transportation afterlife.

tl;dr Miriam Wells is the psychopomp of plane trips.
