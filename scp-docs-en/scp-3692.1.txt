As I'm sensing this is going to be a common theme in criticism of my skip, I feel like I'm obligated to address it.

I'd encourage anyone reading this and having the same thoughts regarding 3999 to reread that scp and my own. 3999's author and myself use a similar style (which, I should note, has been for 100+ years and is kind of the zeitgeist of postmodern literature) to very different end goals. We have fundamentally different stories, different statements to make on. If 3999 is Finnegan's Wake, this is House of Leaves. If 3999 is Gravity's Rainbow or Infinite Jest (closer to Pynchon than Wallace due to 3999's abundance of dreams/stream of consciousness) this is theMystery.Doc, or John Dies at the End. I could go on. Was Danielewski aware of and influenced by James Joyce? It certainly seems that way - Joyce undoubtedly pioneered a style of deconstructionist literature, his influence is vast and cannot be understated. But being the first (not that Joyce was, either) does not give one a monopoly on an idea or structure. 3999 is as influenced, at least apparently (I won't speak to the author's intent, but  anyway), by the authors I list in my scp as I am by them and by it. Furthermore, 3999 and 3692 are saying very different things on fiction, identity, emotion, and other concepts. At least in my interpretation of both.

In light of criticism, however, I may take her down and spend some more time working with her - this might be  but all documents are living - but I still stand by my idea.

So is this  thing. That's up for y'all to decide. I just find issue with surface similarities in 1/4th of my article seemingly becoming synecdoche for the deeper meaning of this piece as a whole.

Your commentary is obviously valid - what I'm saying has no more inherent worth as an interpretation than your own. I am curious about the typos you mention, however.

Regarding your criticism about the suspension of disbelief - it's supposed to be on shaky ground to begin with. Not shattered entirely, so for you at least, I failed there. But clearance Levels 2 and 3 have more things separate from them aside from the blue bits and un-redacted info (, the computer's inconsistent replies to invalid commands, and a lot other smaller easter eggs) - which are meant to be hints that something isn't right. Then when June relives Jamie's death, that's when they break.

The overarching concept (well, one layer) of this is that June is writing the whole thing, computer terminal and all, in the notebook, in an attempt to cling to their identity and their reality, which fails as real life and other fictions bleed in. They then fade away when I stop writing them/when they stop writing this article (hence ). My biggest issue writing this was putting enough hints into the first two articles to build up to the third, without showing my hand too early. Balancing the build-up/escalation with a marginal appearance of normalcy was complicated, to say the least.

I'm of the same mind as some of the other people who posted, but I'd also note that some of the formatting decisions made it hard for me to read (this is possibly specific to me, but I'd imagine it's not.) Interviewed:  SCP-3692-1, Dr. Jamie Omensen, 27-year old junior researcher recently diagnosed with [DATA EXPUNGED].

Interviewer:  Dr. June Rhodes, Site-██ administrator and senior researcher of SCP-3692.
附加记录：    

would read much better as

Interviewed:  SCP-3692-1, Dr. Jamie Omensen, 27-year old junior researcher recently diagnosed with [DATA EXPUNGED].
