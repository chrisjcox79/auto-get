Nitpicks

18 Mar 2020 03:26

I really want to like this article, but there's just a couple things that I'm not vibing with.

I don't think the 'code' itself was justified. If aliens sent us a message, I doubt they'd do it with UTF-8, a totally human construct, using Braille, also a human construct as well as one of the least common languages used with UTF-8. Even if they did know our language, this would be one of the most obtuse methods of communication short of full on encryption.

But, I'm not an alien myself, so benefit of the doubt. Here's the transmission the site would've got.

⠠⠠⠓⠑⠇⠏⠀⠠⠊⠀⠠⠠⠉⠄⠞⠀⠠⠠⠋⠥⠉⠅⠬⠀⠠⠠⠎⠑⠑

I'm no expert on Braille, so forgive any inaccuracies. First, this message is 50ish dots, not 44, although this could just be a case of capitalization or some contraction I'm missing. Second, if the message used Unicode as I've used here, then the translators could've just looked up the codes and found they were in Braille immediately. But then maybe it was ASCII dots. Even then, Braille is arranged in 2x3 dot blocks, not exactly a .

Also, the idea that a nonverbal languages test would not be run after 248 attempts for a message explicitly in written format is a little absurd. I understand the decryption team is supposed to be a bit daft but it's ridiculous that such a suite of analyses for an alien message wouldn't include anything not uttered through vocal cords.

Also, there's a bit of an issue with the comments; some of them are explicitly written after the test, such as #2, and some explicitly written before, such as #249. It wouldn't make sense for #249's comment to be written in that way if the comment can be added or changed after the test itself.

Also also, it's sometimes unclear what  even means. #3 is measuring complexity, and it just fails, implying the language is too complex to even measure. But Braille is super simple, debatably more simple than our written alphabet. #247, measuring whether the message is art, also fails, but it's totally unclear whether that means the message is art, if it isn't art, or if the system couldn't figure it out.

But you know what, still funny. +1

22 Oct 2020 05:38

I'm no expert at braille, but from my limited experience I would transcribe a short message like this without any caps marks (⠠⠠)

⠓⠑⠇⠏⠀⠊⠀⠉⠄⠞⠀⠋⠥⠉⠅⠬⠀⠎⠑⠑
附加记录：    

but that works out to 41 dots.

⠠⠠⠠⠓⠑⠇⠏⠀⠊⠀⠉⠄⠞⠀⠋⠥⠉⠅⠬⠀⠎⠑⠑
附加记录：    

works out to 44 dots, and with proper caps, though I'm not sure if the pasage caps sequence (⠠⠠⠠) is used properly here.

I imagine that it's printed out in an ASCII-art sort of style; the old BSD UNIX utility ppt(6), which prints out a picture of a punched paper tape comes to mind. Calling ASCII art UTF-8 art isn't technically wrong though, but it's kind of awkward.

There are a large number of regional and language-specific braille variants; even if you knew it was braille, analyzing text to find the right variant one sounds like a nightmare. They all do have the same 6- or 8-dot structure, though

All that being said, this does seem surprisingly realistic, if the dots are all smashed together. It's a short enough message (with close to 0 repititions) that measuring its complexity would be pretty difficult, and I don't think most people would recognized a 3x88 bitmap of braille dots as braille just by looking at it.

Anyway, great twist, +1
