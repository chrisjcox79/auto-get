09 Jul 2013 12:56

If a man turns into a murderous wolf-human hybrid once every 28 days it doesn't necessarily follow that he is allergic to silver. Assuming that you have a werewolf of myth without testing could get people killed.

Even in the most clear-cut cases it doesn't necessarily follow that the legend is true. Maybe that broken-winged humanoid constantly weeping blood actually is a formerly divine fallen angel, cast out of a literal heaven by a literal YHWH, or maybe it's just something that wants you to think that. Or maybe it's just a mutant that ancient men found once and made up a story about.

Good science dictates that they not jump to conclusions, no matter how , until they have some basis for them.

Fold

01 Aug 2013 22:35

I agree that it would be bad science to jump to conclusions about what many objects appear to be, but is it reasonable to assume that these parallels would be completely ignored? The possible biblical/historical implications of Cain, Able, and Lilith are never acknowledged in their respective articles. There are also cases like SCP-746 where the mythological parallel could reasonably have been used as a much simpler visual cue for in-universe readers (), but were not mentioned at all.

At minimum the old myths would make a reasonable frame of reference for testing: no, we should not assume that the wolfman has a silver allergy based only on folklore, but we sure as hell SHOULD be investigating the possibility. The fact that we're not doing so for the most part strikes me as very strange, but is also one of the most interesting quirks in the Foundation, especially when an article like this pops up.
