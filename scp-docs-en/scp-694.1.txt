Fold

21 Sep 2015 20:54

When developing a loop SCP, you need to expand on it emotionally. This is because you have a strict set of rules and controlling actions dictating what's going on, without leaving much room to expand on it. So every bit counts. I've been guilty of this too, even.

I forget the number, but Dinner With Andrew by ObserverSeptember does this wonderfully by breaking out of the on-rails experience and letting the characters think and feel for themselves while still stuck in an ongoing Hell, and what they learn coming out of it makes it all the more powerful. 176, on the other hand, doesn't let us sympathize with the people trapped; all we know is that their Hell lasts a few brief seconds, gunned down and burnt by an explosion. The looks on their faces (or rather, the words describing their looks) tell us more than any dialog from them could.

Here, you set up a few narrative building blocks, but don't expand upon them in a way to characterize or make the reader emotionally connected. The father is clearly a reality-bender, the two kidnappers deliberately have these nicknames for some reason, and it's the dude trying to lead them astray to save his son. But the dialog hampers the emotional connection by making them sound like bad movie parodies (and no, meaningful name choices don't justify this unless it is an anomalous movie played out in real-life) and making the father sound overwrought. Finally, I can't fathom why the father would want to relive a traumatizing event over and over without doing anything meaningful to stop it. It snaps my suspension of disbelief.

The experiment logs also tell me nothing that you don't already imply and that the audience can't already infer. It takes up space to try and make the article feel more weighty. The amount of content isn't the issue so much as the quality of it.

21 Sep 2015 23:08

Hey man, I really enjoyed your new work. It flowed pretty nicely and was fairly easy to understand. So many of the articles I've been reading lately have been kinda boring or so wordy it's easy to get lost, but I think you really hit the nail on the head.

+block

–block

The concept seems so familiar, where were you inspired? It feels like something I saw out of the outer limits or twilight zone. I really liked the reveal toward the end, where the father pulls the rabbit out of the hat and leaves the reader with more questions than answers. I had an idea for an SCP like this a few years ago, but of course never worked it out. In mine, the spheres of repeating time caused the subjects to unknowingly loop back on each other, where one future event was actually in the past of the previous event for one subject, but in the future of the other. The timeline basically looked like a pretzel. I eventually dropped it because some TV show or some movie at the time came out with the same premise a month or so later.

I like how yours was more straight forward and a lot more easier to understand than mine would have been. However, the 'no one can avoid' fate mechanic at the end felt very forced and a bit unsatisfying. The route I would have gone with was one that made each sphere a portal where Foundation personnel could enter and exit but the subjects couldn't perceive or use. The spheres would open and close at very specific times and personnel could have lasting effects on the subjects' timeline, however personnel who were not present at the final bubble's occurrence where lost forever, perhaps lost to a separate diverging dimension from ours. Then the next day, a whole new dimension is created and the entire process begins over again.

You see, in physics, if you rewind time, you rewind with it, rewinding your actions and your memories. You wouldn't know of future outcomes any more than character in a movie know their outcomes if you rewound them on an old VHS tape. The best option, would to create a separate dimension where an outside influence with knowledge of the future could change the present, then there is statistically, at least one dimension where someone from other (out dimension) intervenes and saves the boy. Our time line we live in now would be a base timeline where you could thread a second timeline through. Out timeline would look like a belt (like the one around your waist) there would be holes in it (the spheres) and the SCP timeline would act like a thread looping in and out of these holes.

Here's where the creepy/sad factor steps in, sure there's a few instances where someone from our timeline intervenes and saves them. But there are many more (one created everyday) where nothing goes unchanged. And each branching dimension is real, with real people in it. There are countless multiverses out there where absolutely nothing changes, and for something to change in one of those created universes, we have do something here in this one. If the wave form never collapses, the Foundation or some other organization would have to spend the rest of eternity righting the same wrong, day after day.
