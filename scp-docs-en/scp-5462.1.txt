The premise? Extremely creepy. The journal entries? Well-written. My problems with this lie with the  connections in the journals. I have to agree with the sentiments expressed here. The references feel unnecessary to me for the most part, and don't go further into any kind of connection that would deepen the intrigue of the story beyond 

Additionally, while I did quite enjoy the logs at first, the sheer number of them and the more or less redundancy of their message made them become a bit of a drag over time. There's very little sense of rising tension across them, making some of the later entries a bit harder to read because they communicate the same message that we've already received: Goya is losing his mind.

Ultimately though, the central premise is very good, and the writing is solid. I just think that this could probably be pared down a bit and better focused.

Fold

21 Sep 2020 08:53

nnnnnnnnnnot the biggest fan of the crosslinks here. it's a shame, because the journal entries display a command of the language that stands entirely well on its own, building towards a horror of its own accord, without the need to lean on the legacies of 701/035 (and awcy???? like what do you even see in the champagne anarchy of AWCY that even suggests baroque Alagadda???????) and the Goya reference is just the cheap whipped cream on top of this cake - the Black Paintings already have so much traumatic weight to them (history! the decrepitude of the body! war!) that to 'enhance' the images with anomalous explanations is to cheapen a lot of their inherent power. what's next - edvard munch was inspired by a certain neck-cracking pissboy??? van Gogh was depressed because of aliens????????????

I feel that a lot more could have been done with this. The power of the language in the journal entries is evident, and the language that the author is so capable of using can very well stand for itself, had it wanted to try, instead of crutching itself with mass references that do nothing to enhance the source materials or the work itself.
