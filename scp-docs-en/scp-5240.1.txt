28 Oct 2020 16:49

It didn't catch my attention right away.  is kind of interesting as a concept, and was decently executed here, but the initually description isn't particularly thrilling.

The log feels like the main draw of the article, the meat of it if you will, but it does seem to drag on for too long. It contains some nice descriptions and interesting worldbuilding, but I can't help but feel it's be more enjoyable and better paced if compressed down to something like 9 or 10 logs instead of 15. The  He said. Instead of - What do you mean? - He said.) And despite the logs being numbered as if they had unseen logs written between them, they don't read like it. They only ever seem to reference the logs we've seen. It isn't too much of an issue, but it's a bit less immersive than it could be.

Spoilers

Iya

In 52, when he describes something in incredible detail then says  off? It would read better if he described it, mentioned taking pills at the end, and then the next log had a line at the beginning about not remembering the previous log at all or being in a state of delerium during its writing. As it's currently written, it feels too real and specific in its description for something that's supposed to be half-remembered as a dream after taking amnestics.

The whole  really that much of a twist? Despite the amount of redactions, it was super obvious right from the moment he found the swastika. I don't think the redactions imrpove the feel of the logs, and it's almost always very obvious what's behind those black boxes anyways. It makes the city feel incompetent if this is all they could do to the logs, or if it was the foundation redacting them, why on earth would they black out direct nazi references but leave more than enough context clues to make it very clear what they're hiding?

And the little note at the end doesn't need to exist, or at least doesn't need to exist where it does, since the foundation is already established as currently being in trade relations with the city at the time of the article's writing, which is clearly after the logs arrived. I think the trade relations with the city should be outlined in the containment procedures, not the description, and certainly not in a nonsensical collapsible note at the end. And if the Ethics committee would really take objection to the foundation dealing with a state that used to be a nazi state and is trying to forget it, I guess the foundation can't ever work with the modern German government because they used to be nazis too, you know.

Overall, it's clear there's a lot of potential and ideas here, but the flaws, to me, outweigh the interesting parts.

I rate -1 with a strong encouragement to the author to keep writing and improving. They can't all be winners.

01 Nov 2020 22:13

The translation falls completely flat because it relies so heavily on the diary entries to build a slow-burning mood and atmosphere. This could only work if the diary entries maintain and intensify the same tone throughout. But they can't. Lines like

This morning I awoke not in my bed in Zone 16, but in a small flat with a very old-timey outfit.
附加记录：    

Today it's kinda violet.
附加记录：    

My curator said that's just working gas from the overworked pneumatic system and that there's nothing to worry about
附加记录：    

This is it - the socialist utopia. Are you sure I'm not in the GDR? No, it's far too backwards - no Trabants, no Spreewaldgurkens.
附加记录：    

continually deflate the tone by sounding incongruous with the rest of the piece. This, combined with a revelation about amnestics that is functionally the same as in SCP-3000, ruin an otherwise interesting take on a dystopian bureaucracy. You could use that as the core of a fresh article outright: for example, a country whose entire citizenry is employed in its government, that tax, pay, and delegate each other to make money by facilitating yet other global black market transactions around the world  and are thus hired frequently by the Foundation. That would be much more interesting than this extremely bloated attempt at dystopia saddled with a cliche story twist about amnestics.
