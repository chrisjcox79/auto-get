If anyone would like to know:

+block

–block

During the American occupation of Japan after WWII, General Douglass MacArthur (who was in charge of the occupation) arranged for legal immunity for members of Unit 731 (wiki link), Japan's bio- and chemical-warfare operation in occupied China. They didn't face war crimes trials for killing tens of thousands of people, and in exchange America got access to their research documents and the Soviets didn't.

I almost went with Operation Paperclip in honor of my home town's patron saint, Wernher Von Braun, but 731 made more sense.

–block

Fold

08 Oct 2014 21:08

Thanks :) Actually no, although the Laundry Files is one of my favorite series.

All the library details are lifted from my old job, and the main inspiration was the thousands of actual yellow-bound conference proceedings we had to deal with. They were a pain in the ass to shelve/move, new ones came in all the time, literally nobody ever read them (because we had digital versions of all of them), but we weren't allowed to get rid of them.

The historical context came later.

Complete tangent: Although I loved the Atrocity Archives and I think Stross pulled it off rather well, I have mixed feelings about anything that tries to attribute a direct mystical or paranormal motivation for atrocities committed in WWII. I like to think that this one is more about unintended consequences than blood magic.
