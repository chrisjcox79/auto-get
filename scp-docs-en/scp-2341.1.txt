This falls squarely in the  is not something that we should be content with.

+issues

-text

I'm becoming wary now whenever I see visual fuckery, because more often than not it's not really demanded by the narrative and is usually present to distract from the writing itself. I'm not saying that people shouldn't use stuff like the Foundation logo watermarks or different formatting for addenda, but it seems a little slick for an organization like what the Foundation is for most writers. I don't think an organization dedicated to secrecy is going to generally plaster official logos all over their reports. Visual presentation should be pushed forward (I'm reminded of McTiriss' efforts in this regard), but not at the expense of narrative or as a cheap means of garnering upvotes. I don't really see the reason it's here, in this case.
Once again, we have a notion among some reviewers that because they don't get what's going on, they must be failing to grasp some key element of the story and give the writer the benefit of the doubt. And again, I must point out that the writer's job is to tell the story. The writer in this case does not even know the story, and it's evident in the writing. This doesn't mean having to spell things out directly all the time (god forbid) or drawing a point-by-point map for the reader, but there should be something to follow. Frankly, throwing things out just to be mysterious for no apparent reason is bullshit, in the truest sense of the word.

~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

+issues

-text

The execution is generally okay up until the last addendum. This is the first time I've seen a  if this in-universe, I would think a picture of the note and a transcription would make more sense. Out-of-universe, there's no reason for it to be formatted like that. And the author bolded a handwritten note?
People have brought up Zachary Callahan already, but really, if this article treads too closely to anything, it's SCP-2110. I generally don't mind if the same subject material gets covered from multiple angles, but this hews way too closely in setup and execution to be considered truly independent of that article's influence, in my opinion. And in an article that lacks meat or characterization like this one does, that really becomes a problem in terms of its value on the wiki.
This may not be a compulsion effect in name, but it suffers from the same problem. There is no relateability here for the reader. No point of view to consider this from. Everyone who gets put in this house, despite it being tailored in a deeply personal manner to them, gets to do the exact same routine, and react the exact same way to it. If it's not a compulsion effect, this actually makes even less sense, since instead of reacting with shock (and in a lot of cases horror given family histories) at the sudden reappearance of childhood settings and dead relations, the subject just sort of go along with some stereotypical family activities. Nevermind if the person actually did these things at any point. People act like robots in this piece, and thus it neither rings true nor does it interest me at all.
The spooky disembodied voices, humanoid monster thing and corpses and whatever all feel like extremely contrived horror tropes that are being hammered into the piece inexpertly, and for no apparent reason. Also, as has been mentioned before, the last note is extremely narmy.

~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

There are cases where there are articles that are bogged down by a by-the-numbers execution, the interesting core of an idea obscured by things like too much attention being paid to self-satisfying format screws or other extraneous elements. This article, however, is really nothing more than a by-the-numbers execution in its entirety. It's essentially a decorated stage with some props and lighting, and nobody's on it.
