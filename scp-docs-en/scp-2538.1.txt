09 May 2016 17:11

This reads to me like a basic, fairly uninteresting idea (magic crime van) with a half-hearted attempt at a backstory stapled onto it. I feel like either the item involved in the article should be interesting enough that its existence challenges previously-held ideas about reality in such a way that it's interesting on its own, or it plays an integral part in a compelling story of some kind.

This story isn't even really developed. It's a mysterious organization trying to steal some dude's magic van. That's pretty much it. And it's full of stuff like this:

With the success of this arms deal, I'm happy to welcome you as one of our traffickers.
附加记录：    

I'm fairly certain that successful criminals don't generally make a habit of leaving recorded voicemails on peoples' phones using the literal terms  Very little about any of this made much sense, and if you're relying on a backstory to carry the article, it's going to need to be a lot better than what's being offered here.

Going overboard with references

26 Jul 2016 06:51

I have to say, as a gamer who had played a lot of PAYDAY 2 in the past, trying to enjoy this article is incredibly difficult. The references got so overboard they ended up distracting me from the story itself.

From the van itself, to the heist equipment (special mention goes to the wacky masks), to the 4-man gang, to a man named Dragan being involved in this situation, and the cheesy phone recordings discussing the progress of the jobs. Everything here is way too similar to be a mere coincidence. To add icing on the cake, there is an exact song in PAYDAY 2 called .

What are references to other media for? Mostly as small gags, irrelevant to the larger situation or covert enough to be overlooked. But filling up an entire scene with overt references and still trying to tell a serious story (without using the skill of Lampshade Hanging to somehow remedy the suspension of disbelief) behind it all is not an effective method at all, as it shatters the audience's suspension of disbelief, making them unable to properly enjoy the work.

If the author was attempting to make a gag-based comedy where suspension of disbelief is not important, such references are fine. But this SCP doesn't seem like a comedy at all, and feels more like a classical SCP story about implications to the larger picture. The references ended up dragging the whole thing underwater.
