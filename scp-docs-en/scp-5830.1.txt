Junior Staff - Site Crit

18 Jan 2020 23:29

kg583, when you post something to the main site, there are two phrases that you acknowledge before posting:

Did you get feedback first? 
附加记录：    

It is your responsibility to post only finished, final work. Site members are not required to justify or explain their votes. 
附加记录：    

The wiki isn't short of resources to improve your work before posting. The following links are for your consideration in the future:

here. This should be your first step.
here.
How To Write An SCP page to review the basics.
The Clinical Tone Declassified page can help you nail that tone needed for a successful article.
Butterfly Squad are dedicated reviewers and can help you flesh out your ideas and strengthen your drafts.
IRC chatrooms to seek out live critique, if that's more your speed.
This in-depth guide on writing your first SCP.
This essay on generic items and how they succeed and fail.
This essay on humanoid SCP objects and how to make them real.

Finally,

–block

I hope that this can give you an idea about the process:

Read a lot. Take the time to read the articles here, and to read stuff elsewhere. Ambrose Bierce wrote some excellent disquieting fiction and created a big chunk of the platform that later horror writers stood upon. HP Lovecraft. Edgar Allan Poe. These are all literary masters for a reason, and while their more archaic style isn't always up to modern standards, the genius of their work is still very relevant.
Take some time to really storyboard out your idea. Look at it from a bunch of angles, see if you can find any inconsistencies or narrative gaps. The links that I have provided above can be really helpful in that.
Get some critique of your idea. There are a lot of people that are really good at what they do that are willing to help if you just give them the time to do so.
Once you have a good idea, write a draft, then give that several read-overs. Write it, then go take a walk or watch a show, then come back and give it another read over. Read it out loud. Read it to a friend.
Once you have a solid draft, get it critiqued by authors here that have experience with the site. Lots of good help in that as well.

附加记录：    

SCPs are inherently works of fiction, insofar as that they serve an ultimate narrative purpose more often than not. If your goals here are not to weave a cohesive narrative in with the SCP format, then you will not have an easy time here.

the Navigation Procedures would be relevant to the study of this SCP, irrespective of its connection to the strange happenings regarding the SCP's discovery.

What you may have failed to realize here is that every word you spend not progressing your story, building characters, adding suspense/atmosphere, or priming your readers is a word wasted. Doing things like this will destroy the pacing of your narrative and cause most of your readers to lose interest.

27 Jan 2020 19:34

This is really a difference in implied creative purpose of this wiki, then. I (and I know I am not alone in this interpretation) read SCPs in the literal sense as a fictitious log of anomalous happenings in our world. It's a reference source, a database of sorts. CIA agents don't write stories into their mission reports, or hope that some compelling narrative pops out of whatever it was they were doing that is more often than not a bit mundane.

That being said, there is the narrative aspect. This is fiction, and fiction tends to tell stories; few would choose to peruse boring mission reports, and perhaps I am underestimating the number of people who read this sort of stuff for the stories more so than the concepts. But, as an extension of my own comment:

I'm not one to want to tailor the SCP to some story
附加记录：    

Instead, I'd rather tailor the story to the SCP.
