Rant over. Thanks for reading (and rating, if you did), and helping me grow as a writer.

Typed this on my mobile with dodgy WiFi so I hope this gets through/doesn't have too many typos.

I downvoted, but I think you have promise. I'd like to see you write more for the site — you'll only improve with practice! You're good at the aspects of writing which are most difficult to learn.

+critique

–order:

I don't see a good reason for the  seems to be a Foundation-only term, I don't see why it should be mentioned in the public domain at all.

 seems a bit cliché and cheap as a basis for a scip, but I suppose that's the jaded, desensitised SCP addict in me talking.

You've used a lot of .

The antisocial/sociopath distinction didn't seem interesting or related to the themes of the underlying anomaly.

You've used a lot of redaction and . Let me put this in a different way: (usually) mystery is a nice breadcrumb trail to a meaty payoff, but it feels dissatisfying to nibble on breadcrumbs all the way through the piece.

The  feels tacked on. It doesn't mesh with the other ideas, mostly because you only have that one-off mention and don't develop it.

I'm going to be mean, so apologies in advance: that dialogue is cringeworthy. When was the last time you heard someone say  stuff is bad too. Imagine you were transcribing actual audio: what reason would you have to type the words you hear in lowercase?

That last D-class addendum kind of makes sense, but is a bit poorly explained.

Here's what I liked.

You obviously have a good idea of what effect your words will have on a reader. This seems a stupidly basic thing to say, what with it being the foundation of any kind of writing, but you read a lot of people's first attempts on this site and it's obvious they've fallen at that first hurdle. You have the ability to get out of your own head and into your reader's, which is a great skill for writing and hard to teach. I can't offer any specific lines as evidence for this, but it is evidenced by another thing I like:

You've structured the piece really well, one-off, undeveloped throwaway ideas aside. The paragraphs flow smoothly, and you've clearly thought about the order in which to present your information to keep the reader interested and unconfused. That highest-level, macro planning of fiction is hard, so well done for getting that down.

Finally, there are a few lines of flair which show you've got a good grasp of language.  was nice and spooky.

Final thoughts: I can tell you're influenced by the plain ol' creepypasta of this site's early days, which is both a blessing and a curse. On the downside, our current batch of SCP writers are more into the complex, thematic pieces that knowingly subvert tropes etc, so you're not going to get much kudos from them by keeping it old school. On the plus side, it's a welcome break from the artsy-fartsy stuff, and a lot of the audience of this site aren't writers. Also, I think you should think a little harder and more critically about the stuff you write, particularly in terms of whether it's too similar to stuff which has been written before, and whether any particular idea/section is strictly necessary or useful for what you've written.

Thanks for the feedback.

response

–block

You don't need to apologize about criticism, I'm pretty aware that dialogue isn't my strong suit. I have a sort of comfort zone when writing characters, and the D-Class  Captain were pretty far outside of it, so it felt sort of weak.

Beyond that, the grammatical stuff (using wrong words) mostly seems to come from the fact that I really never got enough criticism on this sort of writing from people experienced with it, since I always shy away from posting. I'll probably need to look a little closer at that on my next attempt.

Capitalization was a bit erratic, it's a stupid habit of mine I've been trying to break for years.

I guess for the whole [REDACTED] thing, it felt like it was an entity/anomaly that wasn't supposed to be defined by it's appearance. I get it, it's lazy writing, but I dunno. I have a crystal clear image of what they're supposed to look like (written down on a google doc with my other stuff), so it's not a cop-out at least. For the antisocial/sociopathic section, it was definitely more of a plot device. Antisocial was so I had a way to contain the entity without much risk of a reactive event, and sociopathic for the D-17729 log/last collapsible. It's not the best, but I'm not sure how to circumvent that.

Destruction of all memories was a bit unnecessary, but now it's kind of integral for the exploration log, and I can't quite figure out how to get rid of it without the exploration log falling apart. I always saw entities that destroy memories as a sort of self-preservation tactic to avoid being caught/detected. But it's definitely a bit of a throwaway, and in retrospect a couple of ideas like that seem to crop up in the piece.

The last addendum was a pure stream-of-consciousness, but I think that it might need a little work. I just don't know what would be best to change about it for the sake of impact without accidentally ruining it's tempo.

Thanks a bunch for the criticisms. I'm not sure how much I'm gonna tear up in the article yet (I tried to change the basic errors, but I'm pondering how I could amend the D-17729 section's prose.) but I'll try.

I know we're talking edits for after the contest here, but yeah, it's still (as of edit 31) kinda iffy.

+block

–block

Think about it this way: the violence works on two levels. There's the weird/unexpected/gross factor, and then there's the fact that it serves to communicate info about these critters. We can infer from the rest of the article that they follow you but don't like being noticed, or noticing them breaks a  and the sheer violence of their response supports that. The fact that the procedure is so perfectly described and uniform (exactly 117.2 sec, they always do the skin thing etc) suggests that either they are creatures of unchanging habit or inflexible rule-following, or that their response is a ritual intended to accomplish a specific task or send a specific message. The bone-carving and shaping remains into a pattern support these ideas.

So on the information end, what are they trying to accomplish? All I've got is that maybe the bone carving and such is a ritual that results in the erasure. I only got that idea just now, like 5 days later, so it's not exactly front and center. That, or this is just 

On the gross-out/weird end, [DATA EXPUNGED] and [REDACTED] (when used well) are generally placed so that the reader already had a good idea of what's in the box, but leaving them to work out the horrific details. You're already saying the gross stuff out loud, so the expungements aren't doing you much good.

So, basically, it's not hitting either purpose hard enough. I'm not saying you need to 100% spell out the motivations of the beings (you shouldn't) or that there's a particular way I think you should use your redactions. I'm just saying pick your reveals and concealments in this section to maximize both of those factors.
