I hate to always harp on this particular weirdly-common Foundation interviewer tic, but the doctor has been talking to a cat with a tv for a head, one that they didn't even know they could communicate with in the first place, for a minute and a half  before he starts getting mad at it. (I read the conversation out loud and timed how long it took for him to start getting agitated, when he says  if anything, the interviewing doc should be much more supportive and engaged in order to get more information from this bizarre combination of animal and electronics. Conversations with this thing aren't going to be easy, and frankly, the researchers should be happy to be able to have them at all, so I'm not sure why they're so angry about it.

22 Mar 2017 13:34

It's weird and strange - and that's a good thing. It caught my interest and made me read through and through. There's a few nitpicks I have with the clincal tone; such as making a reference to a species (domestic cat) but not giving us binomial nomenclature (felis catus). Otherwise, the containment and descriptions are enjoyable.

However, the interview At the risk of giving conflicting advice I now think you've gone too far in the other direction. Now it seems the researcher is coddling the SCP, but that's my subjective opinion. Here's the line in particular I take issue with:

My emphasis. I don't know it just) Remember, Foundation researchers and doctors have lied before/told half-truths to the SCP about the nature of their containment.

I have a few other nitpicks; having  in parenthesis is, to me, not needed. We can read, of course, that the SCP is doing precisely that. Also, as someone mentioned, if you want to give an interview transcript a feeling of time, in the (silence) portions, you should indicate a time. I.e: [SCP-XXXX is silent for 45 seconds].

All-in-all it held my interest and curiousity to the end. +1 from me. Nothing but nitpicks really stop me from enjoying the article, so well done.
