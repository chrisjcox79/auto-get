If anomalies exist, someone is going to use them to make money.

Faust made a deal with the devil and paid the price. What if you could make a million deals with a million devils — and get away scot-free?

Thanks to AidenEldritch, Shio and 9Volt for giving this a look-over.

Images: I made the  image with Microsoft Publisher.

Because I can’t bear the idea of people not picking up on my countless self-pleasing references:

+wahoo!

–wahoo!

A watchdog body that’s a LITERAL watchdog of Hell, lmao.

Images like these were the inspiration for the case study in this article.

SPV usually stands for something else.

K2 can stand for this thing.

“STC” is pretty close to “SSC”, an irl project that was meant to discover physical truths about reality, never got completed, and whose would-be researchers ended up working in the finance industry.

The “BBB‒” rating was meant to be an analogy to this sort of rating.

Disclaimer: this is very much intended to be a heavy-handed, transparent metaphor for the global finance industry. The Foundation being utterly cowed by this bank is a direct metaphor for real-world regulatory bodies being similarly dominated by the institutions they’re supposed to police. Dell’Avaro joining ITCD from the Foundation is meant to be a direct metaphor for the “revolving door” phenomenon between policer and policed we see in the real world. The “oath subgroups” are meant to be a direct metaphor for real-world tax avoidance schemes and other ridiculously complicated systems set up by financial institutions. The whole of Bielefeld getting consumed is meant to be a direct metaphor for how ordinary people often bear the immense costs of mistakes made by financial institutions. Et cetera, et cetera, et cetera. Oh, and the  summoned with rituals are meant to be literal demons.

The strikethroughs represent revisions; it's a common style thing here on the site, but it's also based on a real-world practice for various types of official documentation:

It started at the time when a correction was made by striking something out in a physical document and inserting any corrections between the lines with a ^ mark, but it's been retained in the typerwriter/digital era because gives both an in-document record of changes and can provide context/further clarification of the change being made.
