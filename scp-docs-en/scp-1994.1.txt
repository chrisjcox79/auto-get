25 Jul 2014 00:13

Tooth horror ought to be a subgenre of body horror at this point. Something about stuff to do with teeth is pretty innately creepy, and this piece operates with a good understanding of that dynamic. In a lesser article, the pictures would have seemed like cheap shock value, but paired with the narrative they really help lend a good sense of immersion to what you're doing.

This also has some nice moves to it. The idea of being unable to eat because you're full of teeth is certainly horrifying enough, but I found the cause of death being systemic, bodily tooth decay to be both clever and nightmarish (those of us that have had a lot of work done are probably cursed with an ability to envision something like that). So you've got originality of body horror going for you, which is always appreciated.

I do have some issues. Like Faraday Cage, I'm not terribly fond of the tooth fairies angle. It detracts from the musty, institutional Eastern Bloc science feel that I love so well, and I find the concept of this dude cutting up fairies to be kind of silly in comparison to the rest of the article. I also could have done without the clearly delineated motivation behind this. Making this about a rich dentist with rich clients who want real teeth gave this a little too much mundanity for what it was, and personally I would have found the recovered logs of a Russian scientist obsessed with teeth for some mysterious reason far creepier in this case. And you walk the line a bit with the experiments; the description of the teenage girl at the end came awfully close to exploitation by my reckoning.

In the end, the craftsmanship that went into this and the solid core concept won the day. This is something that is pretty genuinely horrifying, given extra creepiness by the convincing scientific tone. Good work.

The Tooth Fairy tie-in poses an interesting question. In the initial draft of this, djkaktus was worried that this was too similar to the Tooth Fairy. Apparently between that initial draft, and the final, the writer of the Tooth Fairy gave dj permission to tie it in. So my interesting question is this:

When you are writing a concept that has been done before (pretty well, IMO, btw, that was the first article that I felt I wanted Brain Bleach for), how do you deal with it. I feel that you have a few, no-win options:

1) Don't acknowledge the previous article, and suffer all the 'it's just like the previous article.'

2) Have a nod to the old article, such as 'links between this and SCP-xxxx are being researched,' and suffer the 'you're just trying to milk a previous article.'

3) Fully integrate the previous article into new one, and risk, well, the type of comments this has gotten.

While I constantly quote that 'anything can work IF you pull it off well enough,' I like how this pulled it off. The basic concept doesn't really on the Tooth Fairies, but it adds to the history of the piece, along with tying it in to previously established canon.
