To use a GOC lens here,

At the time that these bioprinters were recovered, shortly following the effective destruction of Prometheus Labs (in the 90s or early turn of the millennium), such advances, particularly within the field of stem cell manipulation and artificial cellular superstructure construction, would be seen as too advanced for the rest of the world to know, and would have been classified as +2Gen: too advanced for the rest of the world to know and utilize at this time. This would've held until recently (within the last 5-10 years), particularly with the expiration of the FDM patent.

In 1989, S. Scott Crump patented the (then-unnamed) Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) process that is common to most 3D printers today1. This provided the Foundation with an effective buffer - with a monopoly on one of the key components critical to the bioprinter concept, any attempt at uncontained replication of the technology was effectively shut down. This gave the Foundation years to test what they possessed, which also matched development of mechatronics and cellular biology/biomedical engineering.

In 2007, Organovo was created2, which specialized in a method of creating 3D biological prints for experimentation, which effectively downgraded the +2Gen rating to +1Gen - advanced, but not so farfetched to seem anomalous anymore. It took years for conventional human understanding to catch up, but in 2007, the knowledge base finally caught up with what the Prometheus Labs researchers were conceiving decades before.

With the expiration of the FDM patent in 20093, other companies began to get involved, effectively turning additive manufacturing as a whole to current generation status (Gen0) and progressing 3D bioprinting itself from cutting edge to more conventional status - still at +1Gen, but becoming more understandable and progressing to Gen0 status. The research put into stem cells and cellular manipulation would also serve to make such devices conventional now.

I have to agree with Roget and Scorpion.

The Foundation is, for all intents and purposes, the Illuminati. They're not against progress, because progress is considered normal, but a catastrophically disruptive tech boom is something to be actively avoided.

Yes, there is a degree of capriciousness to these judgements, and ultimately it's a bald guy at a desk deciding what's okay and what's not, but that's how they roll.

Should they have prevented the development of nuclear weapons? How do you know they didn't contribute to their development to ensure an Allied victory? Sounds like the kind of thing they would do. Preserving Colonialism may have been seen as not worth the effort. Remember, the Foundation is coldly practical. If preserving something will cost them more than it's worth, they're not going to do it. As for Russia/Soviet Union, they likely wouldn't have cared about a peasant uprising in a then backwater country, and the end of the Cold War would have been viewed as a good thing. Again, these are judgement calls being made by a bald guy at a desk, and you may not agree with him, but he's still going to do it.

As for your second point, the Foundation does contain People of Interest, not all of whom are anomalous (Dick C Normus, for instance) and no small number of whom are out for power, political or otherwise. Some anomalies are entire villages or tribes or even societies. Take SCP-752 for instance. They themselves are not anomalous. Deviant from the norm, but not anomalous. Neither they nor their tech are supernatural, but the Foundation keeps their city under lock and key because of how disruptive their existence would be.

Finally, 'Bonkers' may be vague, but in this sense super advanced tech is like porn: hard to define, but you know it when you see it. I stand by my previously made points.
