I liked the initial format screw, and the revelation at the end was terrifying. The problem is, they don't necessarily seem like they belong in the same article, and there's a whole lot of stuff in-between that was a chore to read through. I'm not entirely sure what's supposed to be going on, either. I mean, I'm clear on what happens at the end, but I have no idea what most of the technobabble is about.

Also, there are some serious plot holes with the narrative. For starters, there's a strict rule that the O5s are never supposed to come into contact with anomalous objects. If the method they're using to extend their own lifespans is anomalous, that makes all of them enormous hypocrites, especially since it doesn't really benefit the Foundation as a whole in any way. If the method is non-anomalous, there's no good reason they wouldn't have released it to the general public, or at least to the rest of the Foundation.

More importantly, I find it extremely unlikely that they'd have actually reinstate Roger as an O5 after resurrecting him through anomalous means. They should have contained him right away, or at the very least, given him a strong amnestic and released him from Foundation service (while keeping him under close surveillance). Putting him back in a position of power when he's effectively a revenant just seems like it's asking for trouble.

I think this article would work a lot better if you trimmed it down considerably, and kept a tight focus on the revelation about the true nature of death, since that's the really interesting/horrifying part. Sometimes, less is more.

My problem isn't just that the Overseers are breaking one of their own rules, it's that they're breaking a rule that makes perfect sense and seems necessary for the Foundation's survival. The O5s control the Foundation, and the risk of them being corrupted by some anomalous force is too great. It doesn't make sense that they'd use anomalous objects to prolong their lifespan, because they don't know what side effects might result from the use of said objects. That's what makes the objects anomalous in the first place, they can't be fully understood. How do the Overseers know that the magic potion that keeps them from aging won't also cause them to become radioactive, or result in demonic possession, or implant a subtle memetic command in the back of their minds that wouldn't be noticed until it's triggered?

Also, I feel like the types of people who'd be willing to break such an important rule to extend their own lifespans are exactly the types of people who shouldn't be in positions of power in the first place. As Smapti pointed out, any Overseer who had an  mentality about his role in the Foundation is unfit for the job.

From a narrative perspective, I also think that making the Overseers into immortal superhumans is a bad idea, since it changes the nature of the Foundation from .

Most importantly of all, I don't think that giving the Overseers life extension technology actually contributes anything to the article, especially since the horror of the article is based on the inevitability of death. Much like the format screw and the ridiculously long wall of technobabble, that particular plot point only serves to distract the reader's attention away from the meat of the article.
