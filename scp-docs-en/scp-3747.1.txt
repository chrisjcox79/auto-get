Upon reading this article, I am struck almost immediately by how closely it resembles SCP-1739, from a Dell laptop catalyzing the destruction of another universe to a researcher talking to his alternate counterpart . Unfortunately, the conversation between David 1 and David A is far less compelling, as is the general anomaly.

Furthermore, the SCP-2317-esque escalation fails to grab me, though the subversion of the final object class is commendable. The connection to Gamers Against Weed is unnecessary at best and suspension-breakingly absurd at worst. Finally, the animated text does absolutely nothing to enhance the reading - the fact that you kept the debugging skip tool shows that it actually hinders it.

The effort put into the presentation is admirable; the story being told, however, is not.

I am down-voting - but only just - and here's why:

as ARD says, this is *very* similar to 1739 and a lot of things that this does (even including the coat analogy vs the dog and ball analogy) it does less well than that skip;
this has several tropes - dangerous anomaly hiding behind innocent anomaly, increasing clearance revealing changing object classes - that are pretty old-hat now;
the animated text is unnecessarily flashy - I'd rather enjoy the steak than the sizzle
I found the cross-links incredibly distracting - there didn't seem any need to link this into either GAW or the Xia, and the eventual story (true vacuum caused by an entity, which was captured by a space machine built by the Xia, escapes but somehow(??) gets stuck in the Foundation database, but is then let out accidentally by GAW trying to hack the Foundation) is too convoluted for my suspension of disbelief;
why do the Xia describe the actions of their machine in modern English (Document 3747-34)?
why is level 5 access not allowed to view Document 3747-34 when lower clearances are? or does this anticipate that level 5 staff are required to read multiple levels of documentation to understand an anomaly, which seems inefficient?
it's never made clear how breaking the computer saves the other universe. SCP-3747-B-K already exists in the other universe, and breaking the computer just stops new false vacuums being spawned, according to Graham 2 (not to mention that everything in the other universe - names, passcodes, entire personal histories, the numeric designation of this anomaly - is exactly the same as our universe, except for the true/false vacuum thing, and the blackboxed numeric designation of one other random skip ? unless that's intended to be fridge horror?);
it's also not clear why breaking the computer doesn't release  the evil entity (which is trapped there) rather than killing it;
crucially, the Level 1 and Level 2 versions of the article refer to SCP-3747-A without then explaining what it is. That is either a terribly unhelpful database entry, or a huge failure of InfoSec.

In its defense, this used the iteration format to very good effect, subverting the standard constant escalation and keeping the reader on their toes as to what was really going on. And the writing was good throughout. But it all kinda falls under its own weight by the end, it makes less sense the more you think about it, and 1739 did most of this better.
