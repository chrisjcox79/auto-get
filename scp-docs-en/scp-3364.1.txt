This is definitely not a place where I expected to have this discussion. ^_^ And I admit to having an opinion on the subject.

But this is actually an excellent illustration of how the end run that IUPAC took around the earth sciences actually ends up causing more confusion that it needs to. The question is one of geohistorical dates vs. spans of time. It's subtle distinction, but a necessary one for geology and other earth sciences. And for the last 40 years or so, the convention among those sciences was pretty simpleMa for geohistorical dates and myr (sometimes m.y.) for spans of time. So 3,000 Ma would basically translate to 3,000,000,000 BCE, a specific time on the calendar (which is how I'm using it here), but if you wanted to talk about a quantity of time you'd say 3,000 myr (ago.)

But then in 2011 the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry and the International Union of Geological Sciences formed a joint task force regarding an SI convention for a year, and, without getting sufficient consensus from the earth sciences and over quite a few (in my opinion extremely valid) objections, adopted 'a' as the abbreviation for 'annus,' that is, year, and ka, Ma, Ga for rising powers of it. (kilo-annus, Mega-annus, Giga-annus) The task force really downplayed the importance of differentiating between geohistorical dates and spans of time (again, not enough buy-in from earth sciences!) and suggested that the new convention could be used interchangeably for both.

But then you get issues like this, where my usage of 3,000 Ma isn't discussing a quantity of time, but pointing at a specific time, which is more appropriate in this context. And confusion results.

TL;DR- It isn't that I'm not using SI standard for a given quantity, it's that very recently an SI standard was put into place that uses an abbreviation that already had a well-established use. In this context 3,000 Ma doesn't refer to a quantity of time, but to a specific point in time.
