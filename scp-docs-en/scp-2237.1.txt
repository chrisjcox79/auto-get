Before I get into the article itself, I have to point out some philosophical issues. I don't really care for the line of reasoning espoused by several commenters, in this thread and the previous one when this was an 001, that this is somehow not good enough as a Proposal but good enough for the mainlist.

Just as I think it's erroneous when people say that a piece isn't good enough to be a mainlister, but it's good enough as a -J or a tale, I think the subtext that somehow the bar of quality is different for a mainlist article and a 001 Proposal is in error. For all of this piece's flaws, it was written as a Proposal, a format that has very specific criteria that set it apart from a mainlist entry. The fact that it was ported over into the mainlist format with no changes suggests that the operating assumption is that the bar for  on the mainlist is lower than that for proposals. That is a stance that I reject wholeheartedly. The two formats are different. It's like saying that a novella is inherently better than a short story.

As to the article itself:

+line-by-line

-text

This document has been ratified by the leading members of the American Secure Containment Initiative, Her Majesty's Foundation for the Secure Containment of the Paranormal, the Holy Emperor's Council on Unearthly Matters, and the Tsar's Seers on July 9, 1915, as one of the first documents of the SCP Foundation, a new organization formed from the aforementioned organizations in order to better perform the functions of Securing that which cannot yet be understood, Containing that which would threaten the public's perception of normality, and Protecting the public from threats preternatural or otherwise not yet understood.
附加记录：    

As I said in the chat, I don't care for the fact that disparate powers like the US, UK, Japan and Russia all happen to have Foundation analogue proto-organizations. Even between the two closest allies in that listing (the US and the UK), there were very different worldviews, cultures, and national aims that would have informed how these governments would have gone about dealing with the anomalous. The notion that all four powers just happen to share the idea that this stuff should be squirreled away and kept from public view, especially at a time when they're busy mass-murdering the Central Powers and putting down revolutionaries, flies in the face of any notion of realism on the part of your geopolitical setting.

Euclid (Moderate Containment Difficulty)
附加记录：    

The reminder text seems kind of goofy. There's three (or four) classifications, and even in the early days of the Foundation I'm not sure that's necessary.

In the event of a hostile takeover
附加记录：    

I could be mistaken, but I think  is more of a contemporary business term rather than early 20th century military parlance.

and incorporeal automata capable of speech
附加记录：    

? I have no idea what this is referring to.

■Bachelor's degrees in one or more of the following fields
附加记录：    

Assuming that what you're meaning to convey is that only the best of the best are to be selected for duty here, a bachelor's doesn't make a whole lot of sense. That's basically entry-level qualifications for starting work in a related field, not a high bar for only the most experienced and knowledgable to clear.

As per the Trinity Protocol, the existence of SCP-2237 is not classified; however, all civilians and individuals lacking sufficient security clearance who ascertain the location of an SCP-2237 instance are to be administered Class-C amnestics.
附加记录：    

what? So it's not classified, but anyone that finds out about it gets mind-wiped? That makes no sense at all.
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Conceptually, as I and others have stated previously, this is at heart a reset button. That, of course, invites the comparison to SCP-2000. Now, in and of itself that isn't a problem, though it does mean that one would have to work that much harder to make a retread of such a well-known concept interesting.

This piece, though, really suffers in comparison to that one. Where SCP-2000 sold the reader on the technical capabilities required to pull off such a massive undertaking as rebuilding society after a catastrophic event by balancing realistic detail and brevity, this article just kind of handwaves away the logistical issues. By assuming that we'll have FTL drives, stasis technology, terraforming capabilities that can be localized on a single ship and a rolodex of Earth-like planets in the future, this article loses any real connection to the present-day reader and just becomes a showcase of standard issue sci-fi technology.

The exception of course is the genocidal reality benders. This is apparently the part where this article is supposed to differentiate itself from SCP-2000, but if that's the case, this aspect is woefully underdeveloped. There's no explanation as to what this  mechanism really is aside from a couple of sentences in a memo. There's no acknowledgement anywhere of the scale of what the Foundation is doing in the documentation aside from the aforementioned couple of sentences. And there's no description or viewpoint of any sort from the populations being booted for the sake of humanity, so there's no real horror or pathos because there's nothing to identify with. You've brought out the concept of complete, repeated genocide, but it's not developed even a little. This makes that aspect of the article come across as thin, as well as cheaply treated.

Finally, the article closes with whoever is accessing the file (presumably Bright) ordering a colonization. With absolutely no context or clue as to what is necessitating that. This coda reinforces the overall feeling that this is a collection of undeveloped ideas rather than any sort of cohesive narrative.

Ambition alone does not equate to a successful article, either as a proposal or a mainlist entry.
