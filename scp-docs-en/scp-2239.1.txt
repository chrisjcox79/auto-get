Authorramblings

Marp

Also known as “man, Zyn misses that one neighborhood cat that used to visit her and her friend in the evenings”. Plants are nice and about all I have time for, but cuddling and doting on a plant feels silly and a little sad.

This sort of grew out of a lot of things. One was the thought, “no one will look twice if you see a cat you don’t know walking around and pet it or cuddle it, but no one does that to a person they don’t know”. And yet people find Tesla strange for loving a pigeon.

There was also an article about aquarium sharks I read awhile back that I’d wanted to write something alluding to at one point.

And also there was that video of a swallow crying when its mate died. Animal emotion is something I wonder a lot about. I wonder how those experiences compare to those of humans. Is simplicity preferable? How does depth factor in?

Edit because can't emphasize enough: the interview went through a ton of revisions, with a bunch of reviewers. Mood was changed, it went from blunt to emo teenager to too obfuscated etc. etc. Many thanks for the patience and the advice, you all.

Spoileralert

Alertalert.

It’s a vicious cycle. The less “happy” this thing is, the more animal-like it regresses into acting, because that’s the only way it knows how to get more of that unconditional human affection it has always craved. Being an animal for so long takes its toll.

This poor thing has no control over any of its abilities. It just knows what it wants… or at least it thinks it does.

bitguess.

The thing is only a butterfly bamboo shark that can write kanji at the moment because that's a form ideally matched to its current caretaker (Researcher Kiryu is half Chinese, half Japanese, likes butterflies and sharks, and studies biological anomalies).

The Foundation's seen how many forms this thing is capable of, and now that it can communicate, it's worth keeping the shark in that form instead of testing with various personnel until they get another form that can communicate.
