Mushrooms are not plants. For starter plants, as in Plantae kingdom classification, generally have to produce their own food through photosynthesis. Mushroom, as in organism in Fungi kingdom classification strictly obtains food from somewhere else, sometimes through plant symbiote such as in Lichen, or through fermenting organic materials such as in Natto and moldy bread.

Furthermore, plant cell walls are made of cellulose, compared to most fungi that have chitinous cell walls. The structure of mycellium found in fungal fruit bodies is also fairly different compared to structure of plant matters. Fungal reproduction generally doesn't have sexual system i.e. only one kind of cell exist that could merge with other cell to produce new individual, instead of two kind found in generative stage of most plants.

Regarding the long-term effects of SCP-073 upon soil

01 Mar 2009 07:17

While I understand and sympathize with the desire to employ hyperbole regarding the SCP Series, I feel I must caution my colleagues against it. We should endeavor at all times to maintain scientific skepticism. For that reason, I strongly object to stating that SCP-073's presence makes land "permanently unable to support life. [emphasis mine]". We cannot say that with any certainty; it is beyond our capability to confirm. Rather, we should state that the soil has remained unable to support life for as far back as our records go. I furthermore urge continued testing to determine any possible ways to reverse the effect. We don't want to be caught with our pants down if a similar effect is ever weaponized and used against us! There are a number of SCPs that have not been terminated simply because we fear encountering similar things in future, and we wish to be prepared for any eventuality. I propose a land reclamation project (perhaps using SCPs, sparingly and under controlled experimental conditions. SCP-124 + SCP-914, anyone?) to determine the full extent of SCP-073's ability to make land infertile.
