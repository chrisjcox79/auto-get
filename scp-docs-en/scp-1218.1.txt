I like the idea, but this could use some work.

The tests seem somewhat limited. First of all, I believe that you can defeat one way mirrors by placing an extremely bright light in contact with the glass. Google is failing me, but I remember reading somewhere that it basically lights up the room on the other side enough to see into the other area.

Second, this technology can be used to listen to sounds on the other side of a window by reading the vibrations of the glass. Has this been tried?

Third, what about low-light cameras and image enhancement software? Do they show up on camera?

Finally, on a stylistic note, and this should be taken with a huge chunk of salt, maybe have the test logs end and pick up with a personal diary?

Fold

24 Aug 2012 10:46

To be fair:

The first simply isn't true. If a brightly-colored or reflective surface is right behind the half-silvered surface, it might work; otherwise, the light returning from the other side just isn't powerful enough to ovecome the light bouncing off the near side.

The second is something the author should consider, if only for the sounds (or lack of sounds) in the room beyond. (They haven't noted any mouth movement, after all.)

Image enhancement software has definite limits; as the available information is reduced, the interpolations get more and more approximate. Low-light cameras might work, but only under the same conditions as given in the article; they amplify all of the light.

24 Aug 2012 13:08

The light source thing I'll readily concede, it was something I read long ago somewhere. Although going by the description, it seems that it still might work, and  would still tell the foundation something.

The laser microphone, done right, could be another method to scare the bejeezus out of us - maybe even an audio log? Even if there's no speech, the ambient audio in the viewing chamber could be incredibly creepy.

 as an answer for that test as well.

The Foundation is meticulous and has huge resources, visual observation is where they'd start, not stop.

In addition, the article suffers from a dearth of details, things the researchers would be able to tell even from visual observation:

What are they writing in? Notebooks? Bibles? Ledgers? Clipboards? [REDACTED]?

How big are they? If they're smoking a known brand of cigarette, they'd probably be able extrapolate from there.

Are they fat? Thin? Talk? Short?

Do they have distinguishing characteristics - can you tell one individual from another? Are their suits identical?

Are they watching the mirror like a window, or a TV screen? In other words, do they sit still and watch, or do they bend to see things from a different angle?

These are just off the top of my head. I really like the concept here, but the article needs work.
