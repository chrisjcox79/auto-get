The bit where Euclid is referred to both as “the entity” and as “he/him/himself” is partially intentional. The parts referencing him as a person are supposed to be written by Dr. Krain, such as the note after the first interview and the last paragraph of the document, while the sections that would not be written by Dr. Krain, such as the containment procedures, use the term “entity”. That was a halfterthought though, so I could make it more obvious by more clearly sectioning it, such as making the description either fully use “the entity” or fully use pronouns.

And I suppose explicitly stating Euclid is a male would be better than just implying it with “himself”, so I’ll probably change “human” to “male human”, since that’s a bit more professional than just “man”. Thanks for your advice!

Edit: Yeah, I’ve gotten similar feedback about how the scip could potentially just be a normal person from our time. I put in that statement about “temporal analysis”, but I suppose that is pretty vague and doesn’t really help support the idea of Euclid being from the distant past. I had an idea that this “analysis” was something along the lines of how we can differentiate between steel made after the first nuclear weapons test and steel made before (including steel from ships that sunk before these tests), as the metal made after has small traces of radiation. While that doesn’t really apply in this scenario, I did just think of something that could be used in place of it, so I might add that in soon.

And you are completely right about why I didn’t elaborate on the time travel. Although I suppose it might be beneficial to add in a blurb about how the Foundation has yet to find an explanation for Euclid’s temporal displacement. Anyways, thanks again for your feedback!

Site Criticism Team - Junior Staff Review

25 Jun 2019 23:51

I've chosen to give this a review as a member of the Site Criticism Team since it's been middling between negative and positive rating for a little while.

+[Critique]Access

-[ClickText]

Not much to comment on in regards to the containment procedures here. Concise, leaves enough to the imagination. Nothing too unusual.
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The identity of SCP-4721 was suspected after Dr. Krain, the first researcher to initiate contact, recognized that the entity was attempting to communicate through tapping the sequence of prime numbers.
附加记录：    

That's a rather specific thing to notice. If it were me, I wouldn't know it from any other kind of arrhythmic tapping, especially since it gets a little hazy after thirteen. Even then, just about anybody could be doing this. It's a bit too coincidental on top of a huge leap of logic.
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

This interview is kind of fun. I'm not sure if you needed an interview to get this across, but it does give us a bit of personality and conflict with 4721. I feel that there is more to 4721's interests than mathematics, though. I mean, it's been two thousand years. Surely he had other interests than math?
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

So, we're seeing the conflict realized in this second interview. I'm still a bit baffled that Euclid could have dedicated his entire life and desires to mathematics. It just barely breaks my suspension of disbelief. But I get it. He's the founder. Of course he'd be this invested and frustrated by the advances made.

However, I'm a little let-down by the end of the article. It seems like so easy of a solution, all without a build-up that has any real consequences. So what if Euclid is frustrated by his inability of advanced mathematics? Why should the Foundation care? I guess that's brought out by the O5 vote and the Ethics vote, but it just doesn't feel like it's rewarding to me.
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

All-in-all, I'm not finding myself to be entertained by this. The entity is only anomalous by their nature of being from a different time, and the acclimation into the modern era is unsuccessful, so much so that they just give in and amnesticize him. I don't feel like anything was particularly lost or gained here.

This is a downvote from me. There isn't anything I find remarkable about this piece.

+Response

-collapse

On the bit about the prime numbers, you do make a good point about it being unwieldy. I suppose I can change it to just him repeating the first four prime numbers, since those are about as much as can be easily recognized. And Euclid spent quite a bit of time on prime numbers, even being credited with the proof of infinite primes by multiplying a set of primes and adding one to find a new prime (or product of new primes). That's also why it's just a suspicion, and partly why the researcher asks Euclid who he is instead of asking if he is in fact Euclid.

Part of the reason I wrote Euclid to be so interested in mathematics is that, besides the fact that Euclid did dedicate a significant amount of his time to learning, discovering, and writing about math, it would also be something that he would want to see where it went. Learning that his results were expanded and improved upon, such as how the converse of a theorem he proved was later also proven 1,000 years after him, would definitely have been interesting for him. And there's also the fact that we don't have a lot of information about Euclid's personal life, besides his math writings.

I see what you're saying about the ending not having any buildup. I originally strayed away from having a transcript of the O5 discussion included in the article, but I suppose that would be a good way to actually attach weight to the decision. In my mind, the fact that Euclid did end up being released was fairly monumental, at least in the sense of anomalous human rights. I viewed it as a demonstration of the conflict between parts of the O5 that won't neutralize an anomaly at all if they can help it and parts that view the containment of anomalous humans that could safely and easily have their properties nullified as unethical. I can completely understand how this wasn't well communicated, however, especially since I reduced this conflict and resolution into one paragraph at the end.

Anyways, thanks again for critiquing this article!
