So the concept here is pretty simple really. Though it's very hard to describe. That means that if you're gonna execute it well, you need to make double damn sure that the reader understands what it is very quickly so you can dive into the story you want to tell. I went far too long into reading this without understanding it. Which normally is fine if the concept is something really really interesting and gives me an  moment. But this doesn't do that.

The idea is something that we've seen on the site in multiple skips. There's a bit of a twist on the concept, but nothing outstanding. There's a lot here to describe a very simple idea, and it doesn't feel like it pays off the effort to read it.

The first two sentences of the description, as an example, reference the SCP Foundation using a version of this thing. That's a huge deal. But you don't expand on that. You just say it, and then the story moves to focus on Anderson. The last thing you want to do with a concept this hard to describe is introduce red herrings. Because now you got my mind focused on .

I think, to be frank and honest here, that your best story thread is the Foundation, not Anderson. Of course an GOI or POI is using it this way. There's nothing surprising about that. But the Foundation creating/using something like this that then gets used by others and then gets out of control? That  could hook my interest.

As it stands, this is bland and it meanders. The concept doesn't carry the piece. The Anderson tie-ins don't add to the skip and they don't feel like they add to Anderson. They're just there.

The dialogue works for me. It felt natural, actually maybe a little too casual but that's a personal preference. I don't like strikethroughs but we talked about that in chat. I am eager to see this improved upon, because it has grand potential. Good luck with the piece.

28 Aug 2015 04:21

I'm not really on board with this one, for a variety of reasons. Minor disclaimer, I've read some but not all of the Anderson tales/SCPs, and I didn't/don't intend to click any of the links in this article.

Ok, here we go.

+block

–block

On the first couple readthroughs, I had trouble picturing the concept, particularly what it would look like to an affected person. Even after groking that, I'm still not clear on the transmission characteristics. The interview log was an interesting exploration of the consequences in a specific scenario, but it didn't really help me with either of these issues. The toy-box analogy, for example, didn't really clarify it at all.

The Foundation-originating cognitohazard until you think about it.

The problem is, the way this is implemented is incredibly stupid (in-universe) for several reasons:

1) You're deploying something that is inherently-amnestic resistant and incredibly easily spread on people who are who? Members of the public asking the wrong questions? Deliberate infiltrators who are aware of the Foundation, and thus most likely part of a GoI? I just don't ever see a use case where releasing a ravenous infohazard erasing both connections with and the idea of the Foundation is a good idea. Unless you think you can give it to the West Coast Fifthists every month and hope it doesn't cross-contaminate the GOC, UIU, or U.S. government.

I mean, it prefect for Anderson, but who on earth were the Foundation going to use this on?

2) It's simple enough to modify that (assuming you can perceive it or copy the text) you can smudge out SCP and write anything. I mean, Jesus Christ people.

3) It's perfectly understood (they wrote it), but anomalous enough that when it's not being used by the Foundation, it's considered an SCP. This falls somewhere outside of the categories of demi-anomalous tech (e.g., amnestics, GOC Orange Suits) and secret anomaly use (Thaumiels) into a territory of double-standard I'm not comfortable with. If it was clear that Anderson had modified its effects in any way, maybe. But it's not.

4) How are the Foundation members immune to their version? What if they want to recruit someone already infected?

Ultimately, I could maybe see this deployed as a bureaucratic and all-around terrible idea and later revoked, and then ripped off by Anderson. Maybe. I think it's interesting enough that it wouldn't just be wacky incompetent Foundation hijinks. Unfortunately, that's not explored. We're just supposed to take it at face value that the secretary at Smith  Pool Consultants has 40 of these in their desk.

Also, the final letter. On the upside, it's one of my favorite Foundation-kiss-off notes ever, which is impressive because I usually detest them. On the downside, in the context of the article, I have absolutely no idea what any of it means. As I said, for this article, I'm not clicking links or reading tales, so the note for me has no meaning whatsoever. I'm not talking positive things like an air of mystery or an enigmatic character showing off. It's like a postcard from someone I've never met happened to fall on my SCP dossier.

Honestly, I think I'm gonna downvote. It relies too much on an incredibly flawed central premise (a Foundation device useless to the Foundation but prefect for Anderson), some clarity issues, and sections that rely on external content to their detriment.

I'm with Petrograd on this one - the Foundation uses anomalous items when required, but this seems like a pointless use, and implies one of two things:

1) The Foundation's version of the infohazard is easy enough to modify that a person/group with a focus on robotics was able to manage it - which means that every GoI could do the same, and this one is dangerous.

2) Anderson is highly skilled at creating infohazards, which is well outside their established market.

I could buy a group like AWCY? doing this purely for the art, I could see the Serpent's Hand or Chaos Insurgency doing it as a weapon, and I could see MCD doing it (while immunizing their clients) to maintain privacy. It really doesn't work for me with Anderson, though.
