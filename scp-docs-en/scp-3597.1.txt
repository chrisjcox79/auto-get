Author post

02 Jan 2019 20:20

For Category 2 - Article features a cliché story element.

Clichés:

A highly skilled Mobile Task Force gets steamrolled.
D-Class = Disposable Class

Thanks to wctaiwan, Ensophos for critiques.

+Spoilers

-Spoilers

It's an infohazard that reverses  whenever you work with information about it. As evident in the recovery, being too knowledgable about it (a form of competence) is fatal.

As such, the D-class who attend to it must be very unable to handle it normally, be unable to improve their skill at containing it, and cannot communicate anything substantial about it to the outside world.

What the object actually is is unstated, and is not knowable in-universe.

There is now also a declassification! (also spoilers)

11 Mar 2019 02:20

Your plan to work with expunging a description by having the Foundation throw personnel at it until they can figure out how to contain it isn't a bad idea, and your concept for how the SCP functions is potentially interesting, but in the end the article as a whole feels flimsy.

It's not flimsy from a logical standpoint (as far as I can tell), but a large chunk of what happens following the description is set on detailing a specific and otherwise par-for-the-course series of events in a short timeframe. As a result, it counter-intuitively makes the SCP less interesting, because you try to define it through grounded means. Recovery logs are somewhat of a staple, and it comes off as routine when all the logs effectively do is tell me how the Foundation contain it. I know they also serve to inform the reader of what's going on with the SCP, but that's not the feeling I get when I finish the article.

That said, you know what you're doing because you know why expunged descriptions work and fail, and that you work with your article to make up for the lack of a description. That it doesn't inherently collapse is props to you as a writer. But with articles like Swann's Proposal and 3005 showcasing the narrative angle of expunging a description, and articles like 3942 and 579 showcasing a more clinical and logic puzzle-esque angle, I'm able to see instances of description expunging working in different scenarios. This doesn't successfully land in either angle, so I have to downvote.
