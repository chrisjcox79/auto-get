Nuclear bombs are Safe.

17 Jul 2018 19:35

A while ago, I read an article about how the US government was planning it's long-term radioactive waste dump sites. In particular, what caught my attention was the talk about how they planned to show that the materials are dangerous. Since the radioactive danger will outlast the English language, they have to do so in a way that doesn't use language, and as such they did everything they could to make the places as scary and filled with death iconography as possible.

EDIT:  The article I was thinking of is called This is not a place of honor.

I would like to thank Eskobar) for looking this over before posting.

The nuclear blood serpents are for CadaverCommander

Hun, Ka'a and Óox are Yucatec Mayan for one, two and three. This is a language which still has fluent and native speakers. América, Barcelona and Cáceres are from the Spanish phonetic alphabet  it was NATO at one point, but I decided that Spanish made more sense.

Those would probably be found at a bomb construction site, rather than at a detonation. Nuclear fallout is made up of a whole mess of different radioactive materials.
附加记录：    

I'm the one that suggested that line be changed to what it is now, my reasoning being that the radioisotopes U-235 and Pu-239, used in most atomic bombs (atomic, not hydrogen) stay around for quite a while even after detonation, given that U-235 has a half-life of 703.8 million years and Pu-239 has a half-life of 24,100 years. These isotopes are present both at construction sites (but not to a strong degree, or else someone's violating the local health physicist's wishes) and after detonation.

That said, for U-235, it would be reasonable to also throw in some detections of other long-lived isotopes from its decay chain, given how much time has passed and the fact that a lot of fissioning has already happened (and in accordance with your accurate statement that fallout is ).

+Response

–block

Is there any reason for these designations, instead of, say, -1, -2, and an MTF number? This isn't a criticism, mind you, I just thought these were unusual choices, and I can't see the logic behind them.
附加记录：    

Concerning the subdesignations: The primary reason (which applies both in universe and out) is that codenames are easier to remember and distinguish than numbers.

In the earliest stages of this draft, the subdesignations were Greek letters, which is something that both I and other authors have done before. However, since this SCP is set in Mexico, and all the characters are either Hispanic or Mayan,1 I didn't like the Greek characters and wanted to use something different. I settled on a Spanish phonetic dictionary, which I felt was appropriate.

Yes, it's an unusual choice, but it's something I chose to give it a little more spice, and also based on a general desire to have things be more immediately recognizable.

As for Xi-Kai, that one wasn't my decision: that MTF was created by SCP-3716. I can't say why he chose that designation. I considered crosslinking to one of those, but ended up choosing not to - I still might.

In addition to missing a word, surely the number of rings in the diagram would narrow the range of possibilities greatly?
附加记录：    

I've since clarified that section to really show what I mean by it  basically, while it is a representation of the Bohr model, it's not a very good representation of the Bohr model. The number of valence rings clearly doesn't match up with the depicted size of the nucleus, the electrons are missing, etc.

Those would probably be found at a bomb construction site, rather than at a detonation. Nuclear fallout is made up of a whole mess of different radioactive materials.
附加记录：    

I'm not the most knowledgeable about the exact composition of nuclear fallout, but those two isotopes were chosen in part because their half-lives were long enough that there'd still be some left 1,300 years after an explosion, and because they are used in the construction of a nuclear bomb. (Also because Cyantreuse recommended them.) If you have other isotopes that could possibly be found, please suggest them!

EDIT:  Cyan posted while I was writing this response, I've added a mention that other isotopes formed from the decay chain of U235 were also found.

A good response. Beyond the anomalous use of a subcritical mass, there is little danger here. See the  for more info.
附加记录：    

Pretty much. 4000-América is a rather small collection of nuclear bombs, and none of them are even that big.
