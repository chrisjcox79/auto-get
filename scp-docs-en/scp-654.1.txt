08 Dec 2020 15:07

I teeter between a novote and an upvote. I like the narwhal's anomaly, simple but striking, and I vastly enjoy the escalation of Faeowynn's emails to the Foundation. It recalls SeaWorld's real-life treatment of orcas until recent, and Faeowynn's continued attempts to gain support for Thunderhorn compel me to continue reading.

I don't enjoy the second half as much, though. The interview functions well (though I feel you should remove Everwood's explicit line about bureacracy; given the themes of the work, I understand already that ), but the ending strikes me as not the right fit. The piece evokes the horrors of bureaucracy's ambivalence toward small fry wonderfully in the emails, but the ending makes the Foundation seem actively malevolent to me which, while I understand the ambivalence makes the Foundation seem malevolent, I feel goes a little overboard.

I noticed that the amount WWS needs to pay to keep Thunderhorn contained originally, $15,321.12, goes ignored for the rest of the piece. If the ending sum that the Foundation fines WWS was closer to that number (not exactly, since that'd be too specific), something like $16,000, I think that would be a good callback to that sum, and make the Foundation's ambivalence more apparent as a cause of evil, rather than a symptom of it.

I'll give it a tentative upvote, since I enjoyed the article overall. +1.

EDIT:  Alternatively, you could remove the interview, and make the Foundation's response itself be an email, with the amendment to the Boring Agreement and the fine. That may be more substantial, actually.

This is one of the most incompetent portrayals of the Foundation I've ever seen. Receiving a series of direct emails about a Keter class anomaly that's kicking everyone's ass for months and months, and they can't even manage to acknowledge or send one reply; let alone think to resolve the issue. And it's explained away, not by depicting a small-scale Foundation without the available resources themselves; but by a Foundation apparently just too big and self-important to care.

The fines thing confuses me too. Is this something the Foundation has typically done before? How do they enforce payment of fines; do they have an appeals court for disputed infractions; is there any neutral arbitration? Or is this extortion bit meant to make the Foundation come off as cruel villains in this piece? Because if you're saying that the Foundation were deliberately ignoring the situation as it played out, then this potentially reads to me like some kind of convoluted caroonish scheme to get rid of Wilson's by driving them to bankruptcy. But I'm doubtful that was the intent.

(P.S.: -5° C is below freezing, even for salt water.)
