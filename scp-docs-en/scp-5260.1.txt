This one really hit different. My entire life has been centered around work, where I can succeed, where I fail, what I'm gonna do next, where I'm supposed to end up- and often times I think to myself,  And what happens after is just more and more work until I can't work anymore. And once I can't work anymore, I have no place.

In that regard, I see myself in this D-Class, with his fears of being put away and forgotten, neatly tucked into a little hole so he can be taken care of by nurses and never visited by his family except on holidays, or every few weekends if he's especially lucky. It hurts to see him struggle like this, and his dialogue portrays this perfectly.

I noticed some formatting errors in the interview log, but all in all an enthusiastic upvote.

At first I was turned off by D-919454's rambly speech, thinking it distracted from the anomaly and beating around the bush as to what made it special, but more I read the more I realized that the rambly nature of the dialogue was the point. Given the themes of death and loneliness, the rambly dialogue is D-919454's life of sorrow and fear, not knowing what comes next, before coming to a peaceful conclusion. The dialogue was well-written enough that I was engaged in the man's story, and when he mentioned Beatrice, it finally made sense.
