I'm not really feeling this one, sorry.

The first line of your special containment procedures feels like it belongs in the description, and to a degree, it's re-iterated. It's not actionable, just raw information. If you said  then it might work.

Second, it feels as though you reveal the anomaly too quickly, and too slowly all at once. You spend a great deal of time describing these squid creatures before telling us why they're unique, but then once you do, nothing much else is revealed. The potential twist that they may be intelligent, or at least pack-based predators, doesn't really add much to the story. There's no big twist.

Finally, the dialogue feels realistic, even military, but it's not very engaging. The old cliche of  and a quick emotional tie-in at the end doesn't endear the characters to me at all.

That said, it's all quite well written and formatted, and the core concept has teeth - but without proper execution, narrative, and twist, there's no actual bite. I'll have to downvote for now, but it shows promise.

endearing characters

21 May 2020 22:52

Roughly an hour passes as the Nautilus descends towards the Bathyal zone, stopping periodically for depressurisation. Onboard crew can be heard chatting about personal matters. Communication resumes once the Nautilus reaches a depth of roughly one thousand meters.
附加记录：    

I think writing in some of that personal chatter would help endear the characters to the reader and allow you to establish the relationship between the characters early on.

As they delve deeper into the ocean the reader could delve deeper into their romantic relationship and their struggles.

Maybe their relationship is in a bad place but they have to be professional and work together for the research project. That would add subtext to the silent moments when they weren't talking. In the end when they start talking about how they love each other it could be attributed to the cognitohazard and hint at 4687's process of reproduction.
