13 Dec 2017 13:31

I'm not really sure I get this. The Foundation comes across as comically inept.

The account is either actually anomalous, or the account records are being falsified. I'm going to assume the latter, because if it's actually anomalous then (a) why is it being used for drug money laundering; and (b) the whole drug angle becomes unnecessary.
But if that's the case, why is the Foundation falsifying internal documents that are not made public - there doesn't seem to be a need to do so. If the reason is to confuse internal investigators (such as Gerdinel), then it's a terrible attempt at fraud, as the entries are so obviously bogus that they arouse suspicion. Not to mention the fraudulent account can be found by just asking Ed from accounting (for one site) - is he in on it, and if not, how does he explain the figures? None of this makes sense.
A Site Director admits that the Foundation is willing to kill its own personnel at the request of a celebrity donor - and no-one expects that to be a problem? Isn't that a far greater issue than just drug-running?
Also, the Site Director's blank refusal is almost designed to create more suspicion, rather than defuse it. This is indeed amateur hour stuff.
Moving the drug production to Argentina distances it from Foundation resources, but brings it . The target market can only be the US (no point trying to make money by selling drugs where drugs are already cheap), so that suggests that the Foundation has no/few resources in America. So the Foundation is mainly based in Europe/Asia in this universe? And how does moving production to Argentina protect against discovery? Presumably no-one is about to stumble across a amphetamine production facility with Foundation logos emblazoned on it? And the account was discovered through electronic searches, which can be done no matter where the drugs (or the people doing the searching) are located. So why does it matter where the drugs are produced?

They key component every example you've cited has is thheir feasibility in being an actual entry in the Foundation database, that the Mainlist is supposed to represent. 765 and 1949 are standard format with unorthodox concepts. 579 and 257-ARC feature heavy expungment that isn't unusual for documentation (and much more common for the period they were written in): 257-ARC clearly is a skull, and 579 most definitely follows format while being abstract as SCP-2719. 2763 and 2864 fall under , which has been accepted many times before.

However, this article isn't doing any of the above. It's a selection of internal communication between employees, and not something one would expect to show up on the  at all. The votes do speak for themselves here, though it does show how far we're reaching just for original takes on execution. I'm thinking this needs to be discussed by staff before officially labeling this as a tale I haven't even seen input from Captain Kirby yet.
