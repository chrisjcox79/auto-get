I don't care for articles in which some random person somehow learns on their own to violate the rules of physics, and then uses their newfound godlike ability to make something completely goofy. Other examples of this would be the whiteboards full of dog organs that teach you German. This is the fallacy in the  However, I do get that there are fans of this sort of thing, so this is more of a personal element to my vote rather than a pronouncement of deficient writing.

That said, I do think that the formulaic approach to this is a negative factor. We constantly tell everyone to read the other articles on the site, which is advice that I agree with, but the downside to that is that it reinforces certain elements that appear in a ton of other articles to the point where it just gets silly to see them over and over again. Here, you've got the standard recovery narrative, conveniently extant journal detailing this guy's thought process, and an incident because of course someone is going to blow themselves up. The predictable, move-down-the-checklist feeling I got from this, right down to the officially denied request to use the skip, made this feel less like a standout article worthy of attention and more like a rote execution of something I've seen a hundred times previous.

Even though I don't care for the core idea, it seems like there has to be better ways to tell the story. Does it have be a journal left lying around? Can't they have dug up some video of a sales pitch to investors or some tax filings or some other documentation that would give the reader the information you want them to have? I'm not sure I could come to an upvote on this, but it really could have benefited from pushing against the old established practices a bit.
