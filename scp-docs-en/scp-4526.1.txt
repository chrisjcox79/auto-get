Boy, I hope I remember how to do this correctly.

This is my first attempt at uploading a skip in a long time. I'm trying to do something a little different from my past attempts that worked, but it's not too different. Please bring attention to any formatting errorsthey're probably not deliberate!

I've included some of my thoughts about this piece in the collapsible, but my way of thinking about this is certainly not the only wayfeel free to take it or leave it. Thank you for reading.

+etc.

-skip!

I'll get this out of the way first: yep, in my head, this guy is portrayed by Danny Devito.

My political sympathies are probably clear enough. Still, I hope that I don't lean too hard in forcing an interpretation of Jimmy as truly benign or truly malign. I do think that the Foundation's tendency (in some canons) to cave to the irresistible temptation of using skips (often for very good reasons!) might be akin to opening Pandora's box. Someone's surely explored that in a more full and nuanced way than I did here, but it's a fun question to consider. I personally consider doubt and uncertainty to be a rich territory for dread, even when it grows out of a facially boring question like 

I'm also very intrigued by the idea of a exploring the impact of competing factions, interests, and some of the divides that you see in every other part of society. I hope that some of this came through here in a way that you also consider intriguing and worth the read.

My vow: One day I'll write a skip that doesn't end with two collapsibles that contradict one another.

Inspiration, other than those mentioned in the skip, includes the bold and still-challenging SCP-1851-EX.

Junior Staff - Site Crit

21 Dec 2019 09:53

ETA: The author has made some changes to address my issues, and I have now upvoted this piece. Therefore, the review below is outdated.

I saw this hovering around zero, and decided I'd justify my downvote here.

[+Crit]

–block

Apart from the tonal issues mentioned in previous comments, there are other parts of this that I'm not a fan of.

Firstly, the redaction in the manifestation log doesn't work for me. Redactions and expungements only really work when it's fairly clear what's behind them looking at the context (SCP-4354 is a good example of this). However, due to the only real pattern in these manifestations being the result, I don't have the faintest idea of what's behind the redaction. And the result lacks the sort of absurdity you often see in successful redactions; reinstatement of unions does nothing for me as a reader.

The end also, really doesn't work for me. First off, the presence of the sentence mentioning that the lower class personnel can't open the high clearance note suggests that in your headcanon, personnel can see parts they can't open. In this case, this means that there is a note that the higher ups can see and can't open, and they've just done nothing about it. I don't think that in an organisation like this someone would just be allowed to badmouth the higher ups and get away with it. Looking past that, it's still a fairly generic message. 'The Foundation is bad' is a theme that's tackled a lot, and just narrowing it down to the leadership being bad didn't do enough for me.

I also really don't get what the object class and conproc strikethrough add to the article. There's no addenda mentioning an update in real-time and the presence of these changes adds nothing to the experience of the article, so they just seem unnecessary and led me to expect something that wasn't there.

All these issues considered, I was led to downvote this article.

SoullessSingularity, for any questions or concerns relating to this review.

Thank you, I appreciate the thoughtful critique. The points you take issue with include a few of my nagging doubts when I pulled the trigger on publishing.

+issues.

-clearly!

I hoped that the redacted log would suggest a very large and serious intra-Foundation conflict, when the one right before that wasn’t redacted had to deal with XK-Class scenarios. Ultimately, it’s good to get feedback that an audience didn’t see it as suggesting something big or even something intriguing. The high and low-level addendums were also something that could have been handled with more care: I considered a whole fabricated story like “we need to quarantine any potential cognitohazards and keep this info among the low-level staff only” but was concerned it might have been a bit too much duplicity built into what is, at the end of the day, meant to be technical documentation. You’re also right about the strikethrough.

I’ll think over your comments and think about ways to edit and improve. Thanks again.

My understanding was that the O5 got removed by the other O5s because he did something very bad, and Jimmy was just the one who told them what he'd done. The awkward thing about Jimmy is that his arguments seem to be always *justified*. It's hard to publicly call for the head of somebody who is known to only go after people who are doing something illegal! At least, on those grounds. His knowledge of all the secret files is still a massive security risk, of course.

Not sure that it's about the lower-ranking researchers being *lazy*, more that they're deliberately shielding him because they consider him to be on their side and think the O5s only have it in for him because he spoiled their nasty schemes.

It's still true that that letter at the end is awkward - the idea is that site directors shouldn't have access to it, but in such a context, any director who saw that there was a note on the file that they didn't have access to would immediately want to know what it was and hunt around until they found a low-level researcher who was prepared to spill the beans! I don't know what another reasonably succint way of expressing this would be. Hopefully the author can think up something.
