Fold

21 Jun 2010 11:54

I saw that there was some discussion on upgrading the classification for 253 to Keter in chat while I was at work. As the author of this particular SCP, I'm going to say that I disagree.

The disease's second stage is dangerous, to be sure. The capabilities manifested through the EM manipulation are unexplored. The nature of the intelligence that a swarm infected by 253 could manifest are unknown. Why this particular form of cancer is not only transmissible but seemingly capable of developing radical phenotypes in infected subjects is a question the Foundation would love to answer. These uncertainties lead to a Euclid classification, but Keter? Implicit in the Keter classification is a "scorched earth" policy. Any SCP classified as Keter is something the Foundation would dearly love to neutralize but it cannot. Perhaps the risks of neutralization are too dire, perhaps the thing is just invulnerable.

In its current setting, 253 is much less dangerous then some diseases cultured in labs right now. In its first stage, it burns through hosts. It doesn't hide for years in "Typhoid Mary"-style asymptomatic carriers, and the course of infection IS easily understood. While 253 is tenacious and virulent, but due to the care the Foundation takes as a part of its normal operating philosophy, the actual likelihood of exposure is small. In the event of an accident, by design, outbreaks can be easily contained. Finally, it'd be easy for the Foundation to destroy any extant samples, both of the disease and any current infected hosts. The Foundation, if it moved this to be classified as Keter, would simply destroy 253.

And that, frankly, is part of the theme in this SCP. The Foundation believes itself to be completely in control as regards SCP-253. That confident, controlled, and measured tone (and the implicit sense of hubris and potential disaster if you were so inclined) is part of what attracted me to the site and motivated me to write this (it being my first SCP). Since it was my first, 253 is pretty blatantly designed to pick up on those themes of "risk versus knowledge". My Foundation is a group that would risk the remote possibility of a wild infection to learn more about the disease because perhaps that knowledge could be used to respond to even grimmer threats. 253 is Euclid because the Foundation /hopes/ to learn something useful from it, rather then /resigning/ themselves to a perceived essential need for its destruction. Ultimately, your views on whether this makes the Foundation heroes or fools probably depends on personal philosophy.
