13 May 2015 20:39

I wrote this several months ago and it's been festering in my sandbox all this time, so I figured I may as well take the plunge and post it.

This is very different from the sort of stories I usually tell, and it was very difficult for me to write, because about 95% of it actually happened. It's not often that an SCP can be said to be based on a true story, but this one certainly is.

This story haunted me for a long, long time. Imagine a woman claiming to be your mother suddenly appearing out of the blue and doing everything she can to control your life. Imagine constantly living in fear of strangers attacking you or your family because they were bribed by an obsessed, lonely woman. It's a uniquely terrifying concept for me. Thankfully, those involved have found peace.

oooooooooh. Not good (though in a good way): this reminds me of something

Peppers (may I call you Peppers? :-) ): this could be excellent for all the reasons mentioned above and a few more, but there is a big but, explained below:

+block

–block

- when did the mother (the actual human person, not the SCP) die? Did she die in the US? When did the misery begin: before the human mother died, or after? Is the SCP a mere continuation of the mother's obsession with her daughter? Not clear, IMO

- if the daughter knows her mother is dead, why does she still think that her mother has been/is responsible for her misery? She has been living at a Foundation site for a while; she knows about anomalies. Why doesn't she 'detach' herself from her guilt about her mother, by putting the blame on an anomaly? Is it because the Foundation administered amnestics when she moved out? And if this is the case, perhaps Dr Bostenero should mention somewhere in his letter that he regrets having to do this, but there was no choice

Any attempt to close, transfer ownership, or edit the balance of POI-2190-1's bank account via computerized systems will result in a system error, and any alterations to physical documents pertaining to SCP-2190's wealth will anomalously revert the following day.
附加记录：    

Does this also apply when a 500-dollar wire transfer is made to SCP-2190's latest target? Not clear

Anomalous bureaucratic influence is suspected.
附加记录：    

Can't the Foundation investigate into this? If not 'humanly' (because bank clerks keep forgetting), at least electronically - I mean, like tapping the phones of the bank-clerks, reading their emails, stuff like that

Finally, a personal opinion: I think the daughter's statement is too long, too full of details and events.

But I want to add, concerning this latest opinion as well as the rest of my comments: I apologize in advance if this skip actually describes all or part of something that happened to you, or someone close to you, and if I consequently offended you with this post.

All in all, a good potential skip if only because it makes you think and read carefully.

Sure! Peppers works fine. Thanks a ton for the critique! I've made some adjustments based on your feedback.

+responses

–block

- I've added a line elaborating slightly on the mother's death. The SCP is the mother's ghost, trapped in a supernatural perpetuation of her behavior during life. However, the Foundation can't scientifically prove it's the mother's , so they only wrote that the entity shares her personality and voice.

- I'll leave the nature of the mother's ghost vague in the article itself. It's one of those things that I think, in this case, works better when it's loosely implied than explicitly stated.

- Just because she was held at a Foundation site doesn't mean they told her anything about anomalies. Foundation information is on a need-to-know basis, and she didn't need to know anything more than she already did. Given her background, it's only natural for her to assume that the phone calls from her dead mother are supernatural in nature.

Does this also apply when a 500-dollar wire transfer is made to SCP-2190's latest target? Not clear…
附加记录：    

Yep. Fridge logic. Again, I feel this is a case where the information is implied strongly enough that there's not much necessity in stating it explicitly.

- I changed the line to 

- Much of the daughter's story is true to real events. I'm sorry it was too long for you. I considered trimming the story before I posted this, but I decided that the best way to respect the original events would be to describe them as completely as possible.

Don't worry though—I'm not offended at all! I really appreciate constructive feedback. This may be based on a true story, but it should be subjected to the same level of criticism as any other piece.
