One of the challenges of interweaving narrative into an article is that a narrative needs characters. Your narrative is pretty plot-heavy, but the development of the characters goes only so far as 

I think this is boiling down to a matter of priorities in your writing. You are still spending a lot of time on your basic ideabut it's hard for me to upvote on that alone. That being the case, I have to read through all of your description to get to the morsels of a story that you've left at the bottom. You want your basic idea to be conveyed quickly and to the point, then you can put detail into your narrative.

Observational Site 81-2 has been established thirty (30) km from SCP-2812-1
附加记录：    

This isn't a mistake, per se, but the site has generally gotten away from the  thing, as it's not really necessary.

Fences were initially constructed around the business plaza where SCP-2812-1 is located, but have since fallen into disrepair. The door to the building housing SCP-2812-1 is locked.
附加记录：    

The issues with extraneous stuff in this piece begin here. We don't really need to know about the fences, and of course the door is locked.

SCP-2812-1 is a 1930's style record player
附加记录：    

I feel like there's a better way to describe this. This is a step above .

The player is made up almost entirely of wood, brass, and glass, with other smaller fixtures included (although nothing unusual of a record player of the time).
附加记录：    

Unless that's important to the narrative somehow, I don't think this part is necessary.

The runtime of the track is 5:14, after which there is ten (10) second pause, and then the track repeats itself.
附加记录：    

Not really necessary to include this.

SCP-2812-1 does not appear to be connected to any visible power source.
附加记录：    

You would think that the Foundation could have at least established whether this thing is plugged in or not.  territory.

The primary anomalous effect of SCP-2812-1 is a powerful physical and psychological compulsion apparent in all individuals within SCP-2812-1's range of effect.
附加记录：    

I find that  fails to resonate on any level for me about 99% of the time. There's no qualities about the item itself that would naturally cause any sort of reaction and therefore put force into a narrative, it's just straight-up mind control. I guess mind control is unsettling on some level by itself, but the ramifications of that have been explored in far better detail, here and elsewhere.

The compulsion to get away from SCP-2812-1 seemingly increases as individuals approach the  of the effect.

You may want to adjust the first part of the description to account for this, because I started reading this as the skip affecting all people within its radius (which is what you actually write in that part) and then run into this.

Parts of the town will begin to show signs of being 
附加记录：    

This is a recurring thing with this article. You can't just put prose in quotes and then drop it in a clinical document. The quotes don't suddenly make phrases take on specific meanings such as would be necessary to describe something from a scientific viewpoint.

The effects of SCP-2812-1 seem to be enhanced by SCP-2812-2, and at close range have resulted in severe cerebral hemorrhaging in ██% of those affected.

I don't get why the percentage needs to be redacted here. I know that's a common practice in older articles, but I don't see the actual reason behind it now.

She also seems fairly comfortable with the fact that a strange robot has just showed up to interview her. 附加记录：

As a result of Event 2812-Epsilon, the effective range of SCP-2812-1 has been increased to ██ km,
附加记录：    

Given the lack of understanding of the pseudo-scientific principles at work here, I have a hard time believing that the Foundation conclusively proved that the event itself caused the range to increase. The best it seems that could have been done was to note that the two events coincided.

This one doesn't work for me. I find that there's systemic issues in the execution, for starters, which makes this kind of difficult to get through. There's this weird dichotomy of overly-detailed scientific description that instead of lending an air of authenticity merely lengthens the word count, and lapses into prose that are ineffectively handwaved by adding quotes around them.

I also feel like I'm supposed to find this skip spooky, which, hey, great, I love spooky. The problem is that the tools you're using aren't really up to the job, in my opinion. The record player feels like something out of a stock internet creepypasta, with the working even when not plugged in thing, and I just can't take the monsters you have seriously at all. The  thing is just too goofy for me, and reminds me of Homer Simpson talking about dogs that shoot bees out of their mouth when they bark at you more than anything else.

I do think that  has some narrative force and could be adapted into a workable article. Unfortunately, I don't think this is that article at all.

Kalinin words it better than I could have. Unlike him, I was unfazed by the technical things in his line-by-line, but the article completely lost me at 

Aside from his complaint of inserting prose into quotes in a clinical document (that being a major peeve of mine as well), overall this touches on another big thing that I strongly dislike-homogeny.

Compulsion is mental/psychological. Not all people are identical, not just in terms of psyche but in actual brain chemistry and structure sometimes. If you poke a hole in the exact same spot in 100 people's brains, you're not going to have 100 identical psychological results (other than maybe 100 severe migraines or death by hole in the brain).

Having everyone just universally go  comes across as completely artificial and in no way realistic
