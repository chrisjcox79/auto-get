This just never quite coalesces for me.

+block

–block

The O5s are dead certain of this thing's ultimate intentions, or at least impact. That isn't necessarily a bad thing.

It pumps out toxic creatures that seriously harm humans, and there are so many of them that it will force evolution. Not necessarily a bad thing.

It does strange things to humans. Definitely not a bad thing, although the body horror was way less interesting than them just being there. The best moments on that front were the corrupted D-Class drowning and the Foundation employee on the beach and air bubble, except before I read the specifics in the footnote. The reappearance of Lockwood seemed to ultimately just exist to provide atmosphere, ang it dragged a lot for that.

All of the characters represented in dialogue or writing seemed super concerned with protocol and safety and having solid evidence. Not necessarily a bad thing, a bold choice in fact, but it led to a lot of drag.

Weird incorporeal being fixing stuff? Quite cool, in fact, although the memetic/possession thing got muddled.

Ancient hieroglyphs that sound vaguely like containment procedures? Not new, but definitely not necessarily a bad thing.

But where does it all go? It just reads like this post-mortem, this documentation of a giant manhunt that consistently finds nothing conclusive except for one big dread answer. (I honestly don't know what they expect -2000 to accomplish if the whole biosphere changes.)

There is some horror in futility, and the tragic flaws built into an organisation by their structure and ethos, but the actions here read with their own unique tone compared to other SCPs, and not knowing at all just wasn't satisfying.

When I reviewed this, I said something about the disconcerting nature of the unknown. That unsettling feeling when you see preparations being made, but aren't given an explanation as to why.

The setup to this is astoundingly large in scale. Evacuating the entire eastern seaboard, activating SCP-2000, preparing for a 250 year long rebuilding process, but then very little in the way of why. The logs are vague, the explanations are vague, everything leads towards something nasty without outright saying it. And that's spooky.

There's enough here to leave me unsettled. I don't think this is perfect, conceptually, but it does more than enough to give me pause. I'll be thinking about it for a while.

This is meaty, both in terms of the scientific detail, and the substance of the logs (for which I can appreciate the focus on safety and process, despite the difference in tone they create). There are also some good creepy details - the first half of the exploration, the description of Lockwood's return and the D-Class drowning, in particular.

For me, the article as a whole didn't do enough to drive home these strengths. Part of that comes from the fact that the Containment Procedures give away the ending (the world is toast), reducing the tension of , to a relatively unsurprising translation, some short exploration vignettes that don't add much to the story other than complicating the anomaly further, a few lesions and heart attacks, and ultimately a confirmation of the explanation that could be intuited from the outset. That summary sounds facetious (as summaries can sometimes sound, and for which I apologise) and the individual parts are still well-written, but they felt less like propulsive additions to the overall narrative than additional loose ends.

I suspect that a few more big revisions to this could produce something very strong.

27 Mar 2017 23:54

I appreciate the path taken here of focusing on the effects of the anomaly rather than on its intentions. Explanation isn't a must, and I suspect that doing so might not add much, and could possibly detract from the unsettling nature of the SCP. I feel that the thrust of the article is in the Foundation's attitude to the implications of an end-of-the-world scenario; seeing the use of SCP-2000 as part of the containment procedures in such an as-per-protocol manner is a very strong opener. As other reviewers have expressed, however, there isn't ever really a moment of dreaded revelation or anything that truly stands out as climactic. This feels like a slow-burn horror piece that ends in a quiet, somewhat contemplative fizzle.

There are certainly some memorable bits, in particular the encounter with the entity in the first exploration log (which as a whole is exemplary). There are similarly vivid encounters elsewhere in the piece, but it feels unsatisfying that each individual aspect of the anomaly (the entities, the light, and interior itself) gets so little screen-time. It's almost agonising, because what little is explicitly revealed is golden.
