I enjoyed this, but am no-voting for now.

Myissues:

Myissues:

If any of them are observed communicating with any entity outside the Foundation, all of them are to be terminated, due to the fact that the entity the members of SCP-2422 refer to as “Wandering Father” may pose a security threat.
附加记录：    

This seems pretty extreme. I understand after reading the article the risk posed because they were deliberately inserted into the Foundation, but that's still a pretty extreme reaction. Also, is the containment area out in the open? Is it under a Faraday cage or continuous jamming? I'd like to see some evidence of prevention before the Foundation jumps to execution orders. I mean, they don't want to be forced to kill SCP objects because of a flaw in their own procedure.

- The language in the containment and initial description involving  is way overboard. I get that they're (potentially) not humans, but even for clinical tone, the number of conditional statements is far too high. I nearly stopped reading the article in the second paragraph of the description, and would probably have downvoted there.

附加记录：    

This seems like a break in clinical tone. I understand that it's supposed to be a natural slip-up, but it doesn't feel natural to me, so it looks kind of silly in text.

They're called SCP-2422. I know you prefer to designate your own numbers for the entities in your custody, but I'm afraid I must be rather insistent about this one.
附加记录：    

Is it just because they're recommendation 22? And is there any particular reason the Foundation would go along with this, especially when they believe that these  are a security risk?

All personnel who come into contact with SCP-2422-C are to be reminded not to allow it access to any electronic device.
附加记录：    

This kind of seems like either a revision to the containment procedures or common sense/standard practice. I know that a reminder is appropriate in the circumstances, but this also gets to the point about prevention planning.

I might have expected that kind of shit from a D-Class but never one of our researchers. I am disgusted and ashamed. –Dr. Welsch
附加记录：    

Addendum 2422-2 works fine, and implies this without the note. I understand Welsch's response, but this is neither the kind of thing that would stick around in an official document nor something that is furthering your narrative beyond what is already there.

Edit: Also, the bit with -B threatening the guard. I understand that this was a good way to utilize her ability, but it's kind of melodramatic (Oh no, they're cheating!), and points again to the Foundation not really thinking things through here. I tend to think that if you walk up to a Site and show some level of awareness of the Foundation, you get apprehended, questioned, amnesticized, and released at the bare minimum. Edit edit: MrWrong beat me to it :)

So, overall! The early language almost made me give up entirely. I really like the (pseudo)human drama of the entities, but the Foundation response seems rather wooden (not a huge problem) and their containment procedures a bit non-comprehensive. Even a sentence or two about communication jamming or denying electronic equipment would help there.

As I said, no vote for now. Edit edit edit: Changed to upvote :)

It's an issue that's come up across the site before, and one I'm very sympathetic with. Tim Scriven, who has professional experience in the area, has written a very solid post about it here: SCP's and IQ (Bonus discussion on personality tests as we.

I'm also a graduate student in psychology by trade. Nowadays, intelligence tests are made up of a bunch of subscales investigating various proposed aspects of intelligence, and theoreticians have to be very careful when talking about intelligence to define exactly what they mean and demonstrate that they aren't just measuring other factors. Modern test development is incredibly difficult (especially as we try to eliminate bias and clarify our theories). It's dicey, but it's important work, and we're trying.

If you wanted this to have a bit more modern scientific validity, I'd check out the description of the WAIS-IV here. It's one of the broadest used modern tests, and it would give you an idea of the components involved. You could say something like:

SCP-2422-A: When a human subject establishes direct eye contact with SCP-2422-A, it will gain understanding of the subject’s intelligence level, whether or not it has spoken or interacted with the subject at all. It rates the intelligence of subjects on a scale of 0-35, with the majority of tested subjects falling within 0.1 units of 22. These designations have been found to correlate significantly with most modern measures of intelligence.1
附加记录：    

(Or whatever you want to call the Foundation test, if you want one. I just pulled that one out of my ass.)

If you wanted to get a bit more specific/weird with it, you could pick some of the smaller subscales of the WAIS-IV instead of the broader PIQ and VIQ summaries to imply that he's actually only measuring very specific things. That's probably getting out into the weeds, but you could PM me if you like.

Footnotes
1. e.g., Performance IQ and Verbal IQ ratings of the WAIS-IV, Fluid and Crystalized Intelligence Subscales of the Foundation Personnel Aptitude Test.

First, I'd like to point out that I like how the supplemental materials are structured in this one. Too many times, the article takes a break from the overall narrative to go into some detailed background document or a collapsible or what have you, and it feels like work to have to slog through the article, constantly going back and forth between different parts of the story and severely disrupting pacing. The way you've done this makes for a better reading experience. So that worked well.

The narrative content, however, has profound issues. For starters, there's this:

As a whole, SCP-2422 behaves and interacts similarly to an average middle-class suburban family, with typical alternating congenial and tense internal dynamics observed.
附加记录：    

I get that this is supposed to be designed as some sort of avatar for the family experience as envisioned by American media (more on that later on), but there is absolutely no such thing as the  to the point that you can make scientific assessments about behavioral norms and dynamics. This may come across as somewhat of a nitpick, but it represents both the in-universe Foundation and the out-of-universe author having a fallacious view of what constitutes a family in real life, which misinforms and weakens the rest of the article. To put it more succinctly, you're kneecapping your sense of truth with this mindset, which keeps this from having any sort of thematic resonance.

This reliance on trope and stereotype continues with the focal point of your article, the teenaged daughter. Not only is she  thing up front has already set the table.

 Couple that with yet another use of the Poor Girl in Containment archetype, its own obnoxious stereotype around here, and you've effectively robbed yourself of any ability to rise above cloying, shallow emotional reactions. It's the difference between finding something tragic, and finding it whiny and annoying.

The technical skill with which this was executed is evidently enough to ensure survival, but in my view, the narrative here is completely deficient.
