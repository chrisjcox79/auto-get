15 Oct 2018 15:40

It's fun, but once again I have some problems with the units in use, despite the apparent research.

+block

–block

1/ the model rocket took ten whole minutes to achieve a height of just fifteen metres from the ground? That's a little more than the height of my low-rise apartment block. Up until a few days ago, ladybugs were swarming around it, and could be seen to easily reach a similar height from landing-on-human-clothes altitude in a matter of seconds under their own power. Also, even when we multiply by 240, that's a scale altitude of 3.6km. A pretty disappointing attempt, and a distance that a human could cover on foot in the same amount of time. The world's fastest elevators could cover the same vertical distance in less than four minutes. Unscaled, that's 1.5 metres per minute, or 2.5 centimetres per second - half the speed at which cherry blossoms fall. It would be achingly slow. And as one of the key things about space rockets is that they continually accelerate all the time the motor is burning, that's the average. It would start out much slower, and end up travelling maybe twice as fast. A ladybug in a hurry could probably outpace it up a brick wall on foot.

If they'd reached, say, 15 kilometres, that would be more realistic seeming, though maybe in excess of what is a typical successful flight for a human rocket (scale = 3600km, or nearly 9 times the orbit of the ISS, and a good way towards geostationary), but then that's maybe valid given the flight time. An example modern rocket like the Falcon takes about 8.5 minutes to reach ISS orbit and velocity, which is about 17500mph, or ~7800m/s. Scale that down and we get a fairly reasonable (for a bottle rocket) 73mph or ~32.5m/s. The velocity increase is in truth an inverse square thing, due to the laws of kinetic energy, and in-atmosphere it would be even more limited due to air drag, but if we assume a linear acceleration that gives us an average of 36.5mph, or a shade under 60km/h (=1km per minute). Ergo, that times 8.5 minutes is about 8.5km. For a ten minute burn, somewhere beyond 10km, as the terminal velocity would be higher. So increasing by 3 orders of magnitude from  at least puts us in the right ballpark.

The estimated time for them to reach the moon seems rather similar to how long it took the Apollo missions to cover the same distance - rather than being 240x longer, never mind somewhere in the region of 200,000x - so they must have become capable of going much, much faster in a single technological leap even if the scale speeds are corrected for the initial attempt. An advantage the ladybugs have, of course, is that their smaller size and thus considerable increase in rigidity due to the square-cube law is that they (and their constructions) can withstand much *greater* g-forces than the human equivalents. And a bottle rocket accelerating with the same gee as an Apollo rocket will reach the same, unscaled speeds in the same time. Such a takeoff for the insect astronauts will seem comparitively gentle (compared to, say, falling over the edge of a table and bouncing off a hard floor, which they already take in their stride), but their scale speed will increase 240x faster than it does for a human. Never mind reaching 15km in ten minutes (which would require distinctly fractional g), they could manage a +1g acceleration (hitting nearly 18km in one minute) without even noticing it, and probably +9g (which would cause all but the most hardcore humans to lose consciousness, and is good for almost 10km in 15 seconds) with only mild discomfort.

tl;dr they're going way, way too slow. If anything, they should reach higher unscaled speeds than we can. Square-cube in the house. Same way that elephants would have the devil's own time ever making it to low orbit, let alone any further.

2/ Putting a dome over the entire 1:240 Kennedy Space Centre. That seems excessive. The site itself would measure something like 220 by 40 metres. But launches at the real thing only occur from three places - the main launch pads 39A and 39B, and their recently added, smaller counterpart for light-duty rockets, 39C.

The perimeters of A and B are both about 900m diameter, roughly hexagonal, but the business part is an emplacement of less than 100m across in the centre, with a ~110m tall permanent support structure. C is about half the size in all respects. The pads are relatively close together compared to the expanse of the whole site, but far enough apart (about 2700m between centres) that it would be more sensible to produce a separate dome for each one. If we scale down, that's 3.75m across (about the size of my lounge) and only about 46cm high. I'm not sure whether you can easily get quarter- or half-domes of nearly 4m diameter and 1 to 2m off-the-shelf, but pretty certain you could find hemispherical examples of about 1m diameter and 50cm height without much difficulty, from hydrophonics suppliers, modelling factors, etc. If it came to it, you could get one of those  enclosed habitat things and cut it in half, and immediately have something damn near perfect for the job.

Though that all really seems fairly excessive for the task in hand. Get a couple of regular outline but extra-deep plastic storage crates and upend them over the launch pads, and put an entirely normal sized one over 39C. Stick some rocks or mundane lead weights on top of them. Boom, no more launch capability. If you want to be absolutely certain, give the replica mobile launch platform and the construction building the same treatment.

The rest of the side, extensive as it is, is then of little interest unless the ladybugs build new pads, which can be dealt with in a similar manner as and when needed if even minimal surveillance is maintained. Their workshop for building rockets will have been isolated, as will all the launch stations. The rest of the facility is mainly observation and office buildings, plus a landing strip, and essentially of no interest. Besides which, no other structures are anywhere *near* as tall as either the workshop, launchpad or mobile launcher (and none except the runway and crawlway have anything like the surface area), and could easily be isolated with a series of tupperware boxes, or in the case of the runway half a dozen classroom leadweights placed along its length.

Pretty simple. No need for an over-obvious 220+ metre all-encompassing dome.

3/ Collecting the exploded parts of the rocket, , and then sending it back up? That's not something we personally do, unless there are significant intact parts that can be recovered and exhibit no meaningful damage. It's not worth the risk for a relatively small saving of material expense, as a) the machine was obviously faulty in the first place, b) there may be all kinds of subtle damage caused to it which would then prove fatal to your second attempt. Better to gather the bits, learn what lessons you can from detailed study of them and the apparent failure mode, and build a whole new rocket incorporating those lessons.

I mean, maybe they're low on resources, but plastic bottles are one thing the wilderness currently isn't short of, and stitching one back together from its scattered fragments would be a hell of a job for a human, never mind tiny insects. Just find another lying around that's much like the first one and do a better job of modifying it.

Edit that I was going to commit somewhere in the first section:

Bear in mind the Apollos looked like they took off slowly, but that's only because they were very long, like 100m+. By the time the rocket outlets passed the top of the launch tower, they were already doing considerably more than 100mph. 1G acceleration is already good for 0-60mph in about 3 seconds, but a typical manned launch pulls more like 3G. Scaled down to give the same initially slow appearance, the bottle rocket may take a few seconds to reach human walking pace, but like a freight train it'll keep on accelerating, and a walking human can cover 15 metres quite quickly. It's about the length of a small house plus a token front and rear garden, and typical walking speed is several drinks bottle lengths per second.

Though, please don't take any of this as a problem with the idea itself, or the writeup in general. It's quite delightful otherwise, just the scaling needs attention (and even that seems correct for the size of the bugs at least, as well as the size of a facility that could be easily obscured within a floridian forest).
