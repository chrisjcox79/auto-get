Report any bugs or typos in the Twine choose-your-own-story file here, and I'll fix 'em (there shouldn't be any bugs/typos, but people can't edit Twine files like they can edit wikidot pages, so it has to be me!).

If you grew up reading the same books that I did, you'll recognize a few references, especially near the end of the After Action Report.

I found this comment pretty interesting because it goes to something I thought through a lot before writing. And now get to talk about it!

I found it hard to believe that a hardened MTF member, made to recount the most traumatic event of his life, would have the wherewithal to do so in such a politically-correct manner.
附加记录：    

Sure, this makes sense  except you're missing that the nameless, genderless narrator (hehe) isn't being politically correct (or even 'respectful', for that matter, since usually in this case it's more an issue of respecting another person than being PC). If they were being politically correct, the pronouns would've been dropped, I agree. The narrator is just used to calling Agent Jann Ekua by the correct pronouns, and used to using correct pronouns in general because of Sigma-3 training to blend in with Hand types.

Morecomments

–block

Speaking from experience  that's very difficult at first (even if you are non-binary it's not like you magically automatically use correct pronouns with no difficulty) and then gets easier after a few months of practice. (Still fuck it up in contexts which you're not used to, but that's another story.)

Also: Sigma-3 is the team most likely to be 'ahead of the curve' on this, because they have to deal with people outside the Foundation and 'blend in' with them, and refusing to use correct pronouns due to, say, a military background or traditional beliefs or whatnot, is a good way to fuck yourself over real fast.

(The Hand seem the GOI by far most likely to use alternative pronouns, at least in some factions; ironically they also contain members who would probably hate that more than anyone! This is based on real-life experience with pagan groups female energy and get mad about non-binary/trans people.)

Put another way: they are literally trained to use correct pronouns, because it's part of their cover. In our narrator's case, it's become second nature because the training has worked. It's not so different from 'always speaking in another language if you're trained to'.

If you really have your heart set on gender-less pronouns, though, might I suggest they/them instead of ze/zir?
附加记录：    

I usually choose ze/zir over they/them because then you don't get singular/plural confusion. Plus, in sci-fi and fantasy works in particular, singular 'they' can be more difficult  because you could really have a gestalt intelligence which isn't non-binary but is a 'they', and therefore give the impression that non-binary people are non-human gestalt intelligences.

(Incidentally, that's also why I personally use ze/zir. I don't really care which alternative pronouns people use for me, but ze/zir tends to be easiest.)

I do wonder if it would be interesting to have the narrator comment on that. It would be realistic enough for the interviewer, who might not have Sigma-3 field training, to mess it up, and have the narrator (who hates the interview and therefore the interviewer) correct it. Probably rudely. Unsure about this, though

–block

I have a few major issues with this, some contest related, some not:

The after action report isn't. It's a CYOA thing narrated by a member of the MTF, and only resembles an AAR in that it describes actions that happened after they occured.
I am not a fan of the whole CYOA/Twine thingy. It opens up a new tab in my browser, the formatting is completely different from the wiki style, and it takes a good while to fully load. In short, it's incredibly immersion breaking, which is only made worse by the attempts at greater immersion through the use of second person.
This reminds me of the Dammerung skip, in a very bad way. It seems less like a skip and more like a tale wrapped up in a skip format.

The story here is intriguing, and while I normally prioritize story over gimmicks (used here without negative connotations and for lack of a better term), in this case, the gimmicks weigh down what could have been a great article.
