I met a traveller from an antique land,

Who said—“Two vast and trunkless legs of stone

Stand in the desert . Near them, on the sand,

Half sunk a shattered visage lies, whose frown,

And wrinkled lip, and sneer of cold command,

Tell that its sculptor well those passions read

Which yet survive, stamped on these lifeless things,

The hand that mocked them, and the heart that fed;

And on the pedestal, these words appear:

My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings;

Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!

Nothing beside remains. Round the decay

Of that colossal Wreck, boundless and bare

The lone and level sands stretch far away.”

This was a wild meandering ride, drawing on a multitude of inspirations. It tries to touch on themes of hubris and tyranny, and the defiance before these evils.

For the 4000 Contest, I presented Alexandria Eternal. It only seems fitting to expand upon that canon for the 5000 Contest.

It is as much an exercise in worldbuilding, as it is interested in being a mystery.

I can only hope that you appreciate it.

Requested slots in order: 5001, 5999, 5052, 5250, 5005.

There were also some instances of not very clinical descriptions and a few SPAG errors.

That's not necessarily a problem - 'the ancient aliens did this thing' has been done a billion times - I was just expecting some bigger connection to the real world or Foundation lore to materialise that never came.

Having said all that, I still liked this. It has a neat classic sci-fi adventure feel, without being too derivative and incorporating some interesting themes about faith and loyalty. I think the core idea of an alien race that uses a cognitohazardous language is great - it's original, yet still sounds like something Clarke would have written about, and is simple and easy to understand while still being a distinctly SCP-flavoured thing. +1

Where this links to 4001 is in the disconnect between what the wayfarers expect to find at their destination. They find an uninhabited world, but Nine Solemn Wind first found the world (Earth) because of the presence of a thriving civilization: Homo nobilus, and is very concerned and confused by what their records tell them.

The events of Alexandria Burning and the subsequent reset happen while they're en route to Earth.

The lack of greater consequence and influence is sort of the thematic point. The entire piece is a massive 'fuck you' to its central antagonist, and tyranny and facism in general. It's very deliberate, from the poem from which this piece draws its name, to the ending where Their Majesty's corpse is originally mistaken as just another lump of shrapnel. Their legacy is just a broken wreck lost in the desert.

I do plan on further revising this to clean up parts I'm not satisfied with. There's a lot of untapped potential here and I was rushing to hit the deadline. E10 are deliberately clinical and professional, but there's room to properly develop the familial aspects of P26.
