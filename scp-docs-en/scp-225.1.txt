Megatons is fine. A megaton of TNT is a recognised unit of energy, equal to about 4.2MJ. No need to say .

I'm guessing the Foundation's reasoning is that the worst case scenario would be the total conversion of the mass of both objects to energy, although according to the calculation I just did, assuming the balls are made of solid iron or something of equal density, that would actually yield about 100 gigatons.

That sounds like a heck of a lot, but it's unlikely that this would result in anything even approaching an end-of-world scenario. According to Wikipedia, the 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake released about 9000 gigatons, and the earth is still in one piece.

To do some serious damage, you would need a lot more than that. The asteroid impact that killed the dinosaurs is estimated at about 96 teratons (96,000 gigatons). And that mostly just messed up the biosphere; the only damage to the earth itself was a 180km diameter crater.

To do something that would make  would require thinking much bigger.

earthquakes are a poor measure to use, as most of the energy itself is involved in the relatively minor movement of trillions of tons of the earth's crust as opposed to destruction on the surface. asteroid impact likewise, as most of the force is directed into the ground. a nuclear explosion primarily affects things upon the surface, with the energy directed into the atmosphere and into destructive radiation. a 100 gigaton explosion may sound insignificant compared to the energy of other events(and yes i did these calculations too :P i love this stuff. i worked it out to be an explosion between 60 gigatons and 300 gigatons depending upon what the spheres were made of), but one has to look at the actual effects.

a 100 gigaton explosion would create an area of complete destruction with an approximately 75 mile radius. this would be a fireball 150 miles wide, meaning it would stretch beyond the atmosphere(along with causing seismic effects, though they would be comparatively minor), sending an airblast around the world and blowing a temporary hole in the atmosphere along with sending a significant portion of the atmosphere into space. the overall circle of destruction from the explosion itself would be approximately 1200 miles in diameter, enough to level most of a continent. the tsunamis created would cause massive destruction, and nuclear fallout would be planet-wide.

that said, yes humanity could probably survive it, just the world would be a sucky place to live in the meantime.

meanwhile the  was saying if the two completely failed to annihilate each other, thus referring to the question of what DOES happen when an unstoppable force meets an immovable object. what if both truly have an infinite amount of energy between them and are totally indestructible? then where does the energy go? what happens? this could potentially cause a release of energy powerful enough to wipe out the universe, or create a black hole, or cause reality to cease, or any number of POSSIBLE scenarios. but of course that part is redacted because your imagination for that will be better than my own.
