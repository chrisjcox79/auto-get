22 Sep 2018 06:43

Summoned, I hath been:

My downvote comes in mainly due to what feels like a large amount of bloat here that made my read through it a slog, with the capital offender being the interview log.

The dialogue is one wall-of-text after another; and it's practically straight exposition. There's no real character to the storyteller, at least nothing that feels consistent - wavering between being laid back or conversational and being terrified and unstable, and it doesn't read like it's intentional. Beyond that, it's rife with elisions on what could be the more interesting bits - so all that's there is an excess of vanilla information that reveals practically nothing of note. This could be pared down considerably, or even be cut wholecloth - the only bearing this has on the story's layout is the entity's , which kicks off the final scene.

Speaking of the ending, that's my other main issue with this.

Now, I don't always take issue with all the magic workarounds the SCPF is usually portrayed as using, but the way it's overused here borders on ridiculous, and sapped what tension remained for me. The incessant reliance on things being counterceptual or antimemetic, yet they've all the tools to conveniently and safely show these things regardless, just makes their inclusion feel entirely pointless and immaterial.

Where there could have been evocative visual detail, there's meaningless buzzwords filling in the blanks. What does it even look like, to use  to open a gate?

This leads off with a decent enough 'nom to focus on. They're visually distinct, display interesting behavior, and there's enough detail to hint at intent and complete the package, but this feels like it's overdeveloped all around.

I do like the Fifthist scips that take the  approach. The description of the ship, the idea of souls bound in service beyond their human lifetimes, the hints of either an origin on a parallel earth, or of lives erased from the memory of our world are all wonderfully spooky.

I've gotta agree, though, about the interview, and the log of vocalizations, dragging somewhat. The interviewee seemed too willing to just gleefully exposit. I didn't really read an emotion of, like, desperation to talk to someone, anyone, who could help him out of his situation in the way he spoke to the interviewer, or any kind of suspicion towards people who he thinks had kidnapped him in his past, either of which could have been very natural in this situation. I sort of liked the alternation between moments of clarity and the ability to give straight answers with sections of more incoherent, figurative language, like his consciousness was drifting in and out of connection with this world, but I think this could be more effective with some trimming of the more repetitive parts.
