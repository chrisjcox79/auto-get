P = NP?

27 Jul 2018 13:23

P problems are problems that can be solved quickly regardless of size, sometimes called  problems.

NP problems are : The time it takes to solve them grows with the size of the problem extremely quickly, but once you see a solution, you can verify it easily.

Breaking passwords is an NP problem, and this xkcd shows you how fast they grow. With 28 inputs, it takes 3 days  to break a password at 1000 guesses/second; Add just 16 more inputs, and it takes 550 years . Yet, in either case it's extremely fast for the server to verify that your password is correct. So fast that it doesn't even notice the difference between a 3-days-to-crack password and a 550-years-to-crack password.

The P=NP? problem asks, 

Any time you think,  that's the P=NP? problem. Humor is a perfect example. It's harder to write a funny joke, than it is to hear a joke and know whether or not it was funny. Why? Is there a way to harness the ability to recognize humor and turn it into a way to create humor? If so, then P=NP. If not, then it doesn't.

I once got high with a mathematician friend and I asked him, 

And he said, 

So the P=NP? problem is the question of whether these two groups of problems are actually the same. In other words, Is there something deep in the nuts and bolts of reality that would allow us to solve NP problems quickly?

If not, why is it possible to recognize solutions quickly? Why is it possible to write an algorithm that verifies a password that would take 3 days to crack, and a password that would take 550 years to crack, in basically the same number of milliseconds?

When dozens of people recognize that a joke is funny, but none of them could write the joke themselves, how is that possible? What knowledge are they tapping into that they can all independently laugh at the same funny joke in its final form, but apparently none of them could access when they were trying to write the joke from scratch? What is happening to that extra information?

I like this one a lot. 'Self-important anartist who talks circles round everyone' is a hard character to execute well, but Veronica lands firm on the side of 'fun' rather than 'tiresome'. I like that she catches Hae flat-footed, since it's a good twist on the standard style of interviews with people who aren't supposed to be in the know. I also like how the difficulty of Veronica's statements is just as much a product of her self-absorption and projection as her secret mystic knowledge - it's a good demonstration of how weird her priorities are.

Probably the only problem I have with this is the Veronica shooting herself at the end of the interview. Given her disdain for Hae, it seems weird that her 'checking up' prompts a desire to kill herself immediately. Plus, it's overly pat and feels like it was written in more as a way to terminate that line of inquiry than as a believable character reaction. It'd be a much less grating development if, say, she kicked Hae out and then was found dead sometime shortly thereafter of an obvious suicide. As it is, it kind of makes her feel like a narrative device rather than a person.

As far as I understand it:

+maybe?

-thoughts

We're not meant to fully understand what is going on, because Veronica is self-obsessed and projecting, and assumes Hae is already 'in' on all the information, so she gives a very confused an unsatisfactory explanation for what went down. The basic gist, however, is that the SCP (NP rock as a genre) is the product of an awful internal squabble in a band where A) there was loads of relationship drama and everyone hated each other, and B) several members of the band were powerful mages/cosmic horrors.

My best guess at untangling it is as follows:

Veronica (and possibly Jack) hated Sara and Izzy for unclear reasons, possibly related to Jack being jealous of Sara and Izzy being in a relationship, and possibly because Sara was in some culpability for the death of 'Ashy' (who was clearly important to Veronica). Veronica and Jack decided to create a memetic genre sound that would fill the math rock genre (which Sara loved) with samey imitations, thereby ruining it for her. This appears to have been successful, since Sara killed herself (Veronica possibly stole her soul afterwards, but that might also be dramatic hyperbole). In the aftermath, the band imploded, and Izzy underwent some sort of transformation into some sort of horrible eldritch entity ('Brinegash') that also seems to prevent her from playing music.

Veronica assumes the Foundation Agent (Hae) is a minion of Brinegash/Izzy, and kills herself to avoid whatever she thinks Brinegash might have planned as revenge, bequeathing Sara's stolen soul to both her girlfriend Brinegash and Jack (who may have been infatuated with her).

Brad Cameron was seemingly some lucky bastard who got kicked out of the band in favour of Sara before the band got famous/dissolved into eldritch infighting, but who was still on good terms with Veronica. What precise relevance Ashy, the Goddess of Cider, the Man in Bandages, and Ana have, I don't know (though I suspect Ana is Sara's sister, given they have the same number of letters in their name). I don't think we're necessarily supposed to get much more than this outline.
